    Question 1

    Lesson L01 · Costs

    The long-run cost function c(w,y) gives the:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Cost at a specific output using fixed inputs
    • B) Minimum cost of producing output y when all inputs can be optimally chosen
    • C) Fixed costs only
    • D) Cost of the most expensive production method
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Minimum cost of producing output y when all inputs can be optimally chosen

    LR cost function: c(w,y) = min{wL+rK : f(L,K)≥y}. All inputs are variable in the long run.

    Question 2

    Lesson L01 · Costs

    For f=L^0.5 × K^0.5 with w=4 r=1: the cost function is c(w,r,y) = 2y×(wr)^0.5 = 2y×2 = 4y. Verify that ∂c/∂w = L* (Shephard's Lemma).

    Type: Calculation

    Show Answer

    Answer: ∂c/∂w = ∂(4y)/∂w = y/w^0.5 × r^0.5 = y×(r/w)^0.5. At w=4,r=1: L=y×(1/4)^0.5 = y/2. Check: from optimisation K/L=w/r=4 and f=y → L^0.5×(4L)^0.5=2L=y → L=y/2 ✓

    Shephard's Lemma: ∂c/∂w = L* = y/2 verified.

    Question 3

    Lesson L01 · Costs

    Which property of cost functions ensures that a firm benefits from lower input prices?

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Cost is increasing in y
    • B) Cost is non-decreasing in w (increasing input prices cannot lower costs)
    • C) Cost is concave in w (the benefit of a price fall diminishes with scale)
    • D) Cost is homogeneous in y
    Show Answer

    Answer: C) Cost is concave in w (the benefit of a price fall diminishes with scale)

    Concavity in w: the firm benefits from input price falls (convex isocosts mean substitution helps) but at a diminishing rate — the cost function curves toward the origin in input price space.

    Question 4

    Lesson L01 · Costs

    Why is the long-run cost function homogeneous of degree 1 in input prices?

    Type: Short Answer

    Show Answer

    Answer: If all input prices double, the minimum-cost input combination doesn't change (only relative prices affect optimal mix). With all prices doubled, the cost of the optimal bundle exactly doubles — so c(tw,ty,y) = t×c(w,r,y).

    Proportional price changes don't alter relative prices or the optimal input mix — only scale the cost.

    Question 5

    Lesson L01 · Costs

    A constant-cost industry has a horizontal long-run supply curve because:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) All firms have the same fixed costs
    • B) Entry of new firms doesn't bid up input prices — input markets are perfectly elastic so LRATC stays constant as the industry expands
    • C) All firms exit in the long run
    • D) Government regulates the price
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Entry of new firms doesn't bid up input prices — input markets are perfectly elastic so LRATC stays constant as the industry expands

    In a constant-cost industry, input prices are unaffected by industry expansion (inputs are not specialised). So LRATC stays at minimum and the LR supply is horizontal at min LRATC.

    Question 6

    Lesson L02 · Costs

    A competitive firm's supply function y*(p,w) is derived by:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Setting price equal to average cost
    • B) Setting price equal to marginal cost — the portion of the MC curve where P≥AVC gives the supply function
    • C) Minimising average cost
    • D) Setting MR equal to average cost
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Setting price equal to marginal cost — the portion of the MC curve where P≥AVC gives the supply function

    Competitive supply: P=MC (profit max condition). For P≥AVC the firm produces; for P<AVC it shuts down.

    Question 7

    Lesson L02 · Costs

    For c(y) = y² + 4 with P=$6, find profit-maximising output and profit.

    Type: Calculation

    Show Answer

    Answer: y*=3, π=$5

    MC=2y=P=6 → y*=3. π=P×y-C=6×3-(9+4)=18-13=$5.

    Question 8

    Lesson L03 · Costs

    In an increasing-cost industry the long-run supply curve slopes upward because:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Firms become less efficient at larger scales
    • B) Entry of firms bids up input prices (e.g. land, specialised labour) raising LRATC — the industry can only expand by offering higher prices
    • C) New firms have higher costs than incumbents
    • D) Government imposes supply restrictions
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Entry of firms bids up input prices (e.g. land, specialised labour) raising LRATC — the industry can only expand by offering higher prices

    Increasing-cost industry: inputs are scarce and specialised. As the industry expands, competition for inputs raises their prices, pushing LRATC upward.

    Question 9

    Lesson L02 · Costs

    Show that for a competitive firm with c(y)=y²+F the supply function is y*=P/2.

    Type: Calculation

    Show Answer

    Answer: MC=2y=P → y*=P/2

    Setting P=MC: P=2y → y*=P/2. This is valid for P≥AVC=y (i.e. P≥0 always for positive output). Shutdown if P0.

    Question 10

    Lesson L02 · Costs

    By the envelope theorem the profit function π*(p,w) is convex in p. What does this imply for supply?

    Type: Short Answer

    Show Answer

    Answer: Convexity of π in p implies ∂y*/∂p = ∂²π/∂p² ≥ 0 — supply is non-decreasing in price. Higher prices induce firms to supply at least as much. This is the formal proof that supply curves slope upward (or are flat).

    Convexity of profit in price → non-negative supply elasticity.

    Question 11

    Lesson L03 · Costs

    Short-run industry supply is the:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Supply of the largest firm
    • B) Horizontal sum of all individual firm supply curves (MC curves above AVC)
    • C) Vertical sum of firm demand curves
    • D) Average of all firm supply curves
    Show Answer

    Answer: A) Supply of the largest firm

    Industry supply = sum at each price of all firm quantities. Each firm's supply is its MC (above AVC). Horizontal sum gives the industry curve.

    Question 12

    Lesson L03 · Costs

    An industry has 100 identical firms each with supply y_i = P/2 (for P>0). Find industry supply and equilibrium P if market demand is Q=500-50P.

    Type: Calculation

    Show Answer

    Answer: Y=50P; Eq: 50P=500-50P → P=$5, Q=250

    Industry supply: Y=100×P/2=50P. Setting 50P=500-50P → 100P=500 → P=$5. Q=50×5=250.

    Question 13

    Lesson L03 · Costs

    A decreasing-cost industry has a downward-sloping long-run supply curve. This can occur because:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Firms become more efficient as they grow
    • B) Industry expansion creates positive external economies — e.g. a larger industry generates specialised suppliers or knowledge spillovers that reduce all firms' costs
    • C) Government subsidises the industry
    • D) Firms collude on price
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Industry expansion creates positive external economies — e.g. a larger industry generates specialised suppliers or knowledge spillovers that reduce all firms' costs

    External economies of scale: as the industry grows, supporting infrastructure and specialised suppliers develop, lowering costs for all firms — LRATC falls with industry output.

    Question 14

    Lesson L03 · Costs

    Explain the distinction between short-run and long-run industry supply responses to a demand shock.

    Type: Short Answer

    Show Answer

    Answer: Short run: existing firms move along their MC curves — output rises, price rises, firms may earn economic profit. Long run: entry attracts new firms (or exit occurs if losses), expanding (contracting) industry supply until economic profit returns to zero. LR supply is more elastic than SR supply.

    In SR, only intensive margin adjusts (existing firms). In LR, extensive margin also adjusts (entry/exit).

    Question 15

    Lesson L03 · Costs

    Australia's mining sector experienced a resources boom 2003-2012. Using industry supply analysis explain the long-run supply response.

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Supply stayed flat — mines can't expand
    • B) In the SR prices rose and existing mines expanded output (movement along SR supply). In the LR new mines entered attracted by profits, shifting supply right and moderating price rises — an increasing-cost industry response
    • C) All mines shut down during the boom
    • D) The AUD fell during the boom
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) In the SR prices rose and existing mines expanded output (movement along SR supply). In the LR new mines entered attracted by profits, shifting supply right and moderating price rises — an increasing-cost industry response

    Resources boom: SR price rise → entry → LR supply shifts right. Australia's increasing-cost resources industry: land/labour bids pushed LRATC up as industry expanded.

    Question 16

    Lesson L04 · Costs

    Producer surplus in a competitive market equals:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Total revenue
    • B) Revenue minus variable costs — the area above the supply curve below price
    • C) Total profit including fixed cost recovery
    • D) Fixed costs only
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Revenue minus variable costs — the area above the supply curve below price

    PS = TR - TVC = area above supply curve below price. Differs from profit by fixed costs: π = PS - FC.

    Question 17

    Lesson L04 · Costs

    With supply P=2Q and demand P=20-2Q find equilibrium and calculate producer surplus.

    Type: Calculation

    Show Answer

    Answer: P*=\(10, Q*=5; PS=\)25

    Eq: 2Q=20-2Q → 4Q=20 → Q=5, P=10. PS = 0.5×base×height = 0.5×5×10=$25 (triangle above supply curve).

    Question 18

    Lesson L04 · Intermediate

    Why does producer surplus overstate profit when fixed costs are large?

    Type: Short Answer

    Show Answer

    Answer: PS = TR - TVC. Profit = TR - TC = TR - TVC - TFC = PS - TFC. When fixed costs are large, PS can be positive while profit is negative — the firm covers variable costs but not fixed costs. PS only equals profit when FC=0.

    Question 19

    Lesson L04 · Costs

    A competitive equilibrium maximises total surplus (CS+PS). This is because:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Prices are always low
    • B) All mutually beneficial trades occur: every unit where WTP ≥ MC is produced. Any restriction (e.g. monopoly, tax) creates deadweight loss by leaving beneficial trades unmade
    • C) Consumers always win in competitive markets
    • D) Producers earn maximum profit
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) All mutually beneficial trades occur: every unit where WTP ≥ MC is produced. Any restriction (e.g. monopoly, tax) creates deadweight loss by leaving beneficial trades unmade

    At P*=MC every unit where value (WTP) exceeds cost (MC) is traded. No beneficial trade is foregone — total surplus is maximised.

    Question 20

    Lesson L04 · Costs

    Australian iron ore producers earn significant producer surplus (quasi-rents) during price booms. In the long run this surplus tends to:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Persist permanently
    • B) Attract entry and investment eroding the surplus as supply expands and costs rise (increasing-cost industry)
    • C) Disappear immediately
    • D) Be captured entirely by workers
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Attract entry and investment eroding the surplus as supply expands and costs rise (increasing-cost industry)

    In LR competitive equilibrium entry eliminates economic profit. For a natural resource (fixed land) true rents persist; for a reproducible input, quasi-rents are competed away.

    Question 21

    Lesson L05 · Costs

    In long-run competitive equilibrium economic profit equals zero because:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Firms are inefficient
    • B) Free entry attracts firms when profit is positive; free exit occurs when profit is negative — the process continues until P = min LRATC and π = 0
    • C) Government taxes all profit
    • D) Firms collude to share markets
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) Free entry attracts firms when profit is positive; free exit occurs when profit is negative — the process continues until P = min LRATC and π = 0

    Zero profit is the long-run equilibrium condition under free entry/exit. It reflects competitive pressure, not firm incompetence.

    Question 22

    Lesson L05 · Costs

    P = min LRATC in long-run competitive equilibrium. If min LRATC = $30 and demand is Q=1000-10P, find long-run equilibrium quantity.

    Type: Calculation

    Show Answer

    Answer: Q*=700

    LR: P=min LRATC=$30. Q=1000-10×30=1000-300=700.

    Question 23

    Lesson L05 · Costs

    'Zero economic profit does not mean the firm earns nothing.' Explain.

    Type: Short Answer

    Show Answer

    Answer: Economic profit subtracts the opportunity cost of all inputs including the owner's invested capital and time. Zero economic profit means the firm earns exactly its opportunity cost — a normal return on investment. The firm may earn positive accounting profit.

    Normal profit = opportunity cost of capital. Zero economic profit = exactly this normal return.

    Question 24

    Lesson L05 · Costs

    An industry has 50 firms each at min LRATC=$25 producing y*=100 units. Demand rises shifting equilibrium. In the LR what happens to P, number of firms, and profit?

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) P rises permanently; existing firms earn profit
    • B) P returns to $25 (min LRATC); more firms enter; each earns zero economic profit in the new LR equilibrium
    • C) Existing firms expand permanently
    • D) P falls below $25
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) P returns to $25 (min LRATC); more firms enter; each earns zero economic profit in the new LR equilibrium

    With a constant-cost industry: demand increase → SR price rise → entry → LR supply expands until P = min LRATC = $25 again. More firms, same price, zero profit.

    Question 25

    Lesson L05 · Costs

    Rents vs quasi-rents: Australian farmland near Sydney earns high rents. These are:

    Type: Multiple Choice

    • A) Temporary quasi-rents that will be competed away
    • B) True economic rents — the land is in fixed supply so its value reflects location scarcity. These rents persist in long-run equilibrium unlike quasi-rents in reproducible industries
    • C) Normal profits
    • D) Accounting profits only
    Show Answer

    Answer: B) True economic rents — the land is in fixed supply so its value reflects location scarcity. These rents persist in long-run equilibrium unlike quasi-rents in reproducible industries

    Fixed-supply inputs earn true rents that persist even in LR equilibrium. Quasi-rents (returns above variable cost) in industries with reproducible capital are competed away by entry.
