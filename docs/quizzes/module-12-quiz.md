## Question 1

*Lesson L01 · Trade*


Comparative advantage means a country should specialise in goods where it has:


**Type:** Multiple Choice

- **A)** The lowest absolute cost of production
- **B)** The lowest opportunity cost of production relative to other goods
- **C)** The highest wages
- **D)** The most natural resources

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer: B) The lowest opportunity cost of production relative to other goods**

    Comparative advantage is about opportunity cost, not absolute cost. A country has comparative advantage if it gives up less of other goods to produce this good.

## Question 3

*Lesson L01 · Trade*


Even if Australia is more efficient at producing everything, why should it still trade?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Comparative advantage means even a more efficient country has varying relative efficiencies. By specialising in goods with lowest opportunity cost and trading, Australia can consume beyond its own production possibilities — trade expands consumption options for all parties.

    Absolute advantage is irrelevant — only relative opportunity costs determine trade gains.

## Question 7

*Lesson L02 · Trade*


World steel price is $500/tonne; domestic production cost is $700/tonne. Australia opens to imports. Change in consumer surplus if demand is Q=1000-0.5P.


**Type:** Calculation

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Consumer surplus increases by approx $30,000

    At $700: Q_d=650. At $500: Q_d=750. ΔCS ≈ (700-500)×650 + 0.5×(700-500)×100 = $130,000 + $10,000 = $140,000 (using trapezoid method).

The compensation principle in trade theory states:


**Type:** Multiple Choice

- **C)** Tariffs should compensate domestic producers

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer: B) The gains from trade are large enough that winners could in principle compensate losers and still be better off — though this may not happen in practice**

## Question 13

*Lesson L03 · Trade*


Why is a quota generally considered less desirable than a tariff that achieves the same import reduction?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** A tariff generates government revenue (area C) while achieving the same import reduction and market price as an equivalent quota. Under a quota, area C accrues to quota licence holders (often importers), not the government — a transfer with no efficiency advantage.

    Tariffs and equivalent quotas have the same welfare effects except for who receives area C.

## Question 18

*Lesson L04 · Trade*


In 2020 China imposed tariffs on Australian barley and restrictions on wine. What economic risk does this illustrate?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Trade concentration risk: when a large share of exports go to a single partner, that partner can use market access as political leverage. Australia's high dependence on China (~30% of exports) creates vulnerability to politically motivated trade restrictions.

    Diversification of export markets reduces the leverage any single partner has.
