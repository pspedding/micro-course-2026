## Question 1

The long-run cost function c(w,y) gives the:


**Type:** Multiple Choice

- **A)** Cost at a specific output using fixed inputs
- **B)** Minimum cost of producing output y when all inputs can be optimally chosen

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer: B) Minimum cost of producing output y when all inputs can be optimally chosen**

    LR cost function: c(w,y) = min{wL+rK : f(L,K)≥y}. All inputs are variable in the long run.

For f=L^0.5 × K^0.5 with w=4 r=1: the cost function is c(w,r,y) = 2y×(wr)^0.5 = 2y×2 = 4y. Verify that ∂c/∂w = L* (Shephard's Lemma).


**Type:** Calculation

??? success "Show Answer"

    Shephard's Lemma: ∂c/∂w = L* = y/2 verified.

## Question 4

*Lesson L01 · Costs*


Why is the long-run cost function homogeneous of degree 1 in input prices?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** If all input prices double, the minimum-cost input combination doesn't change (only relative prices affect optimal mix). With all prices doubled, the cost of the optimal bundle exactly doubles — so c(tw,ty,y) = t×c(w,r,y).

    Proportional price changes don't alter relative prices or the optimal input mix — only scale the cost.

## Question 10

*Lesson L02 · Costs*


By the envelope theorem the profit function π*(p,w) is convex in p. What does this imply for supply?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Convexity of π in p implies ∂y*/∂p = ∂²π/∂p² ≥ 0 — supply is non-decreasing in price. Higher prices induce firms to supply at least as much. This is the formal proof that supply curves slope upward (or are flat).

    Convexity of profit in price → non-negative supply elasticity.

## Question 14

*Lesson L03 · Costs*


Explain the distinction between short-run and long-run industry supply responses to a demand shock.


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Short run: existing firms move along their MC curves — output rises, price rises, firms may earn economic profit. Long run: entry attracts new firms (or exit occurs if losses), expanding (contracting) industry supply until economic profit returns to zero. LR supply is more elastic than SR supply.

    In SR, only intensive margin adjusts (existing firms). In LR, extensive margin also adjusts (entry/exit).

## Question 18

Why does producer surplus overstate profit when fixed costs are large?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** PS = TR - TVC. Profit = TR - TC = TR - TVC - TFC = PS - TFC. When fixed costs are large, PS can be positive while profit is negative — the firm covers variable costs but not fixed costs. PS only equals profit when FC=0.

## Question 23

*Lesson L05 · Costs*


'Zero economic profit does not mean the firm earns nothing.' Explain.


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Economic profit subtracts the opportunity cost of all inputs including the owner's invested capital and time. Zero economic profit means the firm earns exactly its opportunity cost — a normal return on investment. The firm may earn positive accounting profit.

    Normal profit = opportunity cost of capital. Zero economic profit = exactly this normal return.
