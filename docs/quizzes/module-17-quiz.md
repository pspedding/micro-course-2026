## Question 4

*Lesson L01 · Welfare*


Explain why the 'lens' region in an Edgeworth box represents mutually beneficial trades.


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** The lens is the set of allocations where both consumers' indifference curves through the endowment are satisfied — both consumers are at least as well off as at the endowment. Any allocation in the lens interior is a Pareto improvement over the endowment.

    Both ICs through the endowment bound the lens — trades inside make both better off.

## Question 5

*Lesson L01 · Welfare*


At the endowment MRS_A = 3 and MRS_B = 1 (both = -dx2/dx1). Can both consumers gain from trade?


**Type:** Multiple Choice

- **A)** No — MRS are already equal
- **B)** Yes — they value goods differently so trade can make both better off
- **C)** Only consumer A gains
- **D)** Only if prices are set by an auctioneer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer: B) Yes — they value goods differently so trade can make both better off**

    When MRS_A ≠ MRS_B there are unexploited gains from trade. They can rearrange consumption to both be better off until MRS_A = MRS_B.

## Question 8

*Lesson L02 · Welfare*


For Cobb-Douglas preferences U_A = x1^0.5 × x2^0.5 and U_B = x1^0.5 × x2^0.5 with totals (10,8): write the contract curve condition.


**Type:** Calculation

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** x2_A/x1_A = x2_B/x1_B → allocations on the diagonal

    MRS_A = x2_A/x1_A; MRS_B = x2_B/x1_B. Setting equal: x2_A/x1_A = (8-x2_A)/(10-x1_A) → contract curve is the main diagonal x2_A = 0.8 × x1_A.

## Question 9

*Lesson L02 · Welfare*


Why does the core of an exchange economy lie on the contract curve?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** The core is the set of allocations that no coalition can block. Any allocation off the contract curve can be improved for at least one person — the 2-person coalition can block it. Hence only Pareto-efficient allocations (on the contract curve) in the lens can be in the core.

    Core ⊆ contract curve: Pareto improvements would be blocked by the improving party.

## Question 13

Walras' Law states that:


**Type:** Multiple Choice

- **A)** All prices sum to one
- **B)** The value of excess demands across all markets sums to zero — if n-1 markets clear the nth must also clear
- **C)** Each consumer's budget balances
- **D)** All markets clear simultaneously

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer: B) The value of excess demands across all markets sums to zero — if n-1 markets clear the nth must also clear**

    Walras' Law: Σ pᵢ zᵢ = 0 where zᵢ is excess demand for good i. This follows from budget constraints — allows us to normalise one price.

## Question 14

*Lesson L03 · Welfare*


Explain why the numeraire convention is used in general equilibrium analysis.


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Only relative prices matter for allocation decisions (demand is homogeneous degree 0). Setting one price equal to 1 (the numeraire) removes the indeterminacy of the price level without changing any allocation.

    Absolute price levels are irrelevant; only price ratios determine optimal choices.

## Question 15

*Lesson L03 · Welfare*


If at prices (p1,p2)=(2,1) excess demand for good 1 is +3 units and there are only two goods, what is excess demand for good 2?


**Type:** Calculation

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** −6 units (excess supply)

    Walras' Law: p1×z1 + p2×z2 = 0 → 2×3 + 1×z2 = 0 → z2 = −6.

## Question 19

*Lesson L04 · Welfare*


Why does the Second Welfare Theorem justify redistribution via lump-sum transfers rather than distortionary taxes?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** The Second Welfare Theorem shows any Pareto-efficient allocation is achievable by redistributing endowments and then letting markets operate. Lump-sum transfers don't distort relative prices; distortionary taxes do — they move the equilibrium away from Pareto efficiency.

    Redistribute endowments (lump-sum) to achieve equity; use markets for efficiency.
