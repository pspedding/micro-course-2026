## Question 2

*Lesson L01 · Information*


In Akerlof's model with quality q uniform on [0,1], seller value=q, buyer value=1.5q and average quality sold at price p = p/2: calculate buyer WTP at price p.


**Type:** Calculation

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** WTP = 1.5 × (p/2) = 0.75p < p

    Buyers know average quality of cars offered at price p is p/2 (only sellers with q≤p sell). Buyer WTP = 1.5×p/2 = 0.75p. Since 0.75p < p, no trade occurs at any p > 0.

## Question 3

*Lesson L01 · Information*


Why does adverse selection in health insurance cause premiums to rise even when the insurer is competitive?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** High-premium policies attract sicker individuals (adverse selection). As healthy individuals opt out, the remaining pool is sicker, costs rise, premiums must rise further — a vicious cycle. In Australia, Medicare addresses this via universal coverage; private health insurance uses community rating to limit adverse selection.

    Adverse selection creates an adverse spiral: rising premiums → worse pool → higher costs → higher premiums.

In a principal-agent model the agent exerts effort e and output is y=e+ε. The principal maximises E[y]-w(y). The first-best contract requires:


**Type:** Calculation

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** e* that maximises E[y-c(e)] = e-c(e) where c(e) is cost of effort. FOC: 1=c'(e*) — but principal can't observe e so must use incentive contracts

    First-best: principal observes effort and pays w=c(e*) inducing optimal effort at minimum cost. Second-best: principal can't observe e, must offer incentive-compatible contract.

## Question 8

An efficiency wage is a wage above the market-clearing level. Why might a firm pay this?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Paying above market clears makes workers value their job more (cost of being fired rises). Workers exert more effort to avoid dismissal — moral hazard is reduced because the threat of unemployment is more credible and costly.

    Efficiency wages solve moral hazard by making job loss costly enough to deter shirking.

## Question 12

*Lesson L03 · Information*


For a separating equilibrium in Spence's model: high type's cost of education c_H=1/θ where θ=2; low type cost c_L=1/1=1. Wage difference for signalling: w_H-w_L=$20. Does a separating equilibrium exist?


**Type:** Calculation

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** Yes if high type's net gain > 0: $20 - 1/2 = $19.5 > 0 and low type's net gain < 0: $20 - 1 = $19 > 0... check Riley condition

    Riley condition: high type gains from signalling: w_H-w_L > c_H×e* → $20 > 0.5e*. Low type must not mimic: w_H-w_L < c_L×e* → $20 < e*. So e* must satisfy 20 < e* and 20 > 0.5e* → e* > 20 and e* < 40. Valid separating equilibrium exists for e* ∈ (20,40).

## Question 18

*Lesson L04 · Market Structure*


The winner's curse in common-value auctions means:


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** The winner of a common-value auction is the most optimistic bidder. Conditional on winning, they likely overestimated the value — the act of winning is bad news. Rational bidders shade bids below their estimate to account for this.

    Winning a common-value auction signals you overestimated value — you should bid below your estimate.

## Question 20

*Lesson L04 · Market Structure*


Australia's electricity wholesale market (NEM) uses a uniform price auction. Why is truth-telling not necessarily dominant in this format?


**Type:** Short Answer

??? success "Show Answer"
    **Answer:** In a uniform-price auction all winning bidders receive the same clearing price. Large generators have incentive to bid above marginal cost (exercise market power) since their bid can affect the clearing price for all their output — unlike the Vickrey auction where payment depends only on others' bids.

    Uniform-price auctions create strategic bidding incentives for pivotal sellers — unlike Vickrey.
