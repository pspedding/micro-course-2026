## Learning Objective

Derive the price consumption curve and explain its relationship to the demand function.

---

## The Price Consumption Curve

The **price consumption curve (PCC)** traces the locus of optimal consumption bundles as the price of one good changes, holding income and the other price constant.

As p₁ falls (budget line rotates outward along x₁ axis), the consumer's optimal bundle moves. Connecting all optimal bundles as p₁ varies traces the PCC in (x₁, x₂) space.

**Deriving the demand curve from the PCC:**  
For each optimal bundle on the PCC, read off x₁ and the corresponding p₁. Plotting (p₁, x₁) gives the Marshallian demand curve for good 1.

---

## PCC Slope and Gross Substitutes/Complements

The slope of the PCC reveals whether good 2 is a **gross substitute** or **gross complement** for good 1:

- If as p₁ falls, x₂ also falls (PCC slopes downward): goods are **gross complements** — demand for good 2 moves with demand for good 1. Example: coffee (good 1) and coffee cups (good 2).

- If as p₁ falls, x₂ rises (PCC slopes upward): goods are **gross substitutes** — when good 1 becomes cheaper, consumers buy more good 1 and less good 2. Example: Coles (good 1) and Woolworths (good 2).

For Cobb-Douglas U = x₁^a × x₂^(1-a):  
x₂* = (1-a)m/p₂ — independent of p₁! The PCC is horizontal, meaning goods are neither gross substitutes nor complements in the Cobb-Douglas case.

---

## Homogeneity and the Demand Function

An important property: Marshallian demand functions are homogeneous of degree zero in (p₁, p₂, m). Doubling all prices and income leaves demand unchanged — only real purchasing power and relative prices matter, not the price level.

Formally: xᵢ(λp₁, λp₂, λm) = xᵢ(p₁, p₂, m) for all λ > 0.

This has policy implications: if the RBA targets 2-3% inflation and wages rise proportionally, real consumption baskets are unchanged.

---

## Worked Example

**Petrol price fall and public transport demand in Australian cities**

Let x₁ = kilometres driven (petrol), x₂ = public transport trips, p₁ = fuel cost per km, m = transport budget.

When petrol prices fell 30% in 2020 (COVID demand collapse), if PCC slopes upward (substitutes), public transport demand should fall. PTV and Translink data showed exactly this: car use rebounded faster than public transport after prices fell.

For U = x₁^0.6 × x₂^0.4, p₁ = $0.15/km, p₂ = $3/trip, m = $300/month:  
x₁* = 0.6×300/0.15 = 1,200 km; x₂* = 0.4×300/3 = 40 trips

If p₁ falls to $0.10:  
x₁* = 0.6×300/0.10 = 1,800 km; x₂* = 0.4×300/3 = 40 trips (unchanged — Cobb-Douglas)

The Cobb-Douglas PCC is horizontal — the goods are income-independent (neither substitutes nor complements in this model). Real demand data suggests they are substitutes, requiring a different utility specification.

---

## Common Misconception

> **"The demand curve and the PCC are the same thing."**

The PCC lives in quantity-quantity space (x₁, x₂), tracing bundles as price varies. The demand curve lives in price-quantity space (p₁, x₁). The demand curve is derived FROM the PCC by reading off (p₁, x₁) coordinates for each bundle on the PCC.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The PCC traces optimal bundles as one price varies; the demand curve plots price against optimal quantity
- PCC sloping downward → gross complements; PCC sloping upward → gross substitutes; PCC horizontal → neither (Cobb-Douglas)
- Marshallian demand is homogeneous of degree zero in prices and income
- Australia case: petrol and public transport are empirical gross substitutes — PCC slopes upward
