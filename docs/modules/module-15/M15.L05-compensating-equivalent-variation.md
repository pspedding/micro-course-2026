## Learning Objective

Calculate compensating and equivalent variation to measure the welfare effect of a price change.

---

## Two Welfare Measures

When price changes, we want to measure the welfare gain or loss in dollar terms. Two theoretically rigorous measures exist:

**Compensating Variation (CV)**: How much income must be taken from (given to) the consumer after the price change to return them to their original utility level?

- Price falls: CV > 0 (take away income — consumer gained)
- Price rises: CV < 0 (give income — consumer lost)
- Uses original utility u⁰ as reference, new prices p¹

CV = e(p⁰, u⁰) - e(p¹, u⁰) = m - e(p¹, u⁰)

**Equivalent Variation (EV)**: How much income would give the consumer the same welfare change as the price change, evaluated at original prices?

- Price falls: EV > 0 (consumer equivalent to gaining this much income)
- Uses new utility u¹ as reference, original prices p⁰

EV = e(p⁰, u¹) - e(p¹, u¹) = e(p⁰, u¹) - m

---

## CV vs EV vs Consumer Surplus Change

For a price fall:  
- EV ≥ ΔCS ≥ CV (for normal goods)
- EV ≤ ΔCS ≤ CV (for inferior goods)
- When income effect is zero: EV = ΔCS = CV (quasilinear preferences)

The Marshallian consumer surplus change (area under demand curve) is a convenient approximation lying between CV and EV. For small price changes or when the income effect is small, all three are close.

## Worked Example

**Carbon tax welfare cost for an Australian household**

Cobb-Douglas utility: U = x₁^0.3 × x₂^0.7  
x₁ = electricity (kWh), x₂ = other goods  
Income m = $2,000/month, p₂ = $1  
Original electricity price p₁⁰ = $0.25/kWh  
Carbon tax raises price to p₁¹ = $0.35/kWh

**Expenditure function:** e(p₁, p₂, u) = u × (p₁/0.3)^0.3 × (p₂/0.7)^0.7

Original utility: u⁰ found from x₁* = 0.3m/p₁ = 0.3×2000/0.25 = 2,400 kWh  
x₂* = 0.7×2000/1 = 1,400  
u⁰ = (2400)^0.3 × (1400)^0.7 ≈ 1,704

**CV** (how much can be taken away after tax to restore u⁰):  
e(p¹, u⁰) = minimum expenditure at new prices to reach u⁰  
= u⁰ × (0.35/0.3)^0.3 × (1/0.7)^0.7  
CV = m - e(p¹, u⁰) ≈ $2,000 - $2,082 = -$82/month

The household is $82/month worse off — the CV welfare cost of the carbon tax.

---

## Common Misconception

> **"CV and EV always give the same answer."**

CV and EV coincide only when there is no income effect (quasilinear preferences). For normal goods they diverge: CV uses the old utility level (more conservative welfare loss) while EV uses the new utility level. For large price changes, the gap between them can be substantial.

---

## Key Takeaways

- CV: income adjustment needed at NEW prices to restore ORIGINAL utility — practical for policy analysis
- EV: income change at ORIGINAL prices equivalent to the price change — preferred for comparing multiple policies
- For normal goods: EV ≥ ΔCS ≥ CV for a price fall; the gap grows with the size of income effects
- Treasury and Infrastructure Australia use variants of CV/EV in cost-benefit analysis
