## Learning Objective

Distinguish between signalling and screening solutions to asymmetric information.

---

## Signalling and Screening

Michael Spence's job market signalling model shows how education can signal ability:

- High-ability workers get education more easily (lower cost)
- Firms interpret education as signal of ability
- Separating equilibrium emerges where only high-ability get educated

Screening occurs when uninformed parties design mechanisms to reveal information:

- Insurance companies offer menu of contracts
- Employers use probation periods

Australian applications:

- HECS/HELP student loan system
- Job application processes
- Bank loan screening

Formally, for signalling to work, Riley condition must hold: c_H(e) < c_L(e) for all e, where c is cost of signal.

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Australian universities use ATAR scores as signals:

- High-ability students can achieve high ATAR at lower effort cost
- Universities set admission thresholds that only high-ability students meet
- This creates separating equilibrium where ATAR signals ability

If ATAR became too easy to achieve (costs equalized), it would lose signalling value.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Education only has signalling value"

Correction: Education has both human capital and signalling value. The signalling model shows how even with zero human capital benefit, education could still be valuable.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Signalling comes from informed party (worker)
- Screening comes from uninformed party (employer)
- Signals must be costly in a way that differs by type
- Australian higher education combines both functions
