## Auction Theory

Major auction types:

1. English (ascending bid) - Price increases until one bidder remains
2. Dutch (descending bid) - Price decreases until someone accepts
3. First-price sealed bid - Highest bid wins, pays bid amount
4. Vickrey (second-price) - Highest bid wins, pays second-highest bid

Revenue Equivalence Theorem: Under certain conditions, all standard auctions yield same expected revenue.

Australian applications:

- Spectrum auctions (ACMA)
- Treasury bond issuance
- Online advertising auctions

---

## Matching Markets

Gale-Shapley algorithm solves two-sided matching problems:

1. Propose-and-reject process
2. Yields stable matching (no blocking pairs)
3. Used in:
   - Medical residency matching
   - School choice programs
   - Australian university admissions

Key properties:

- Algorithm is strategy-proof for one side
- Always produces stable matching
- Matching is optimal for proposing side

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Consider Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO) electricity auctions:

- Generators submit supply curves
- AEMO matches supply and demand
- Market-clearing price set at intersection

This is essentially a multi-unit auction where generators are bidders and electricity is the good.

---

## Common Misconception

> "The highest bidder always wins auctions"

Correction: In multi-attribute auctions (like spectrum), winners are determined by both price and non-price factors (e.g., coverage commitments).

---

## Key Takeaways

- Auction format affects bidding strategy
- Vickrey auctions induce truthful bidding
- Matching markets require stability
- Australian markets use sophisticated designs
