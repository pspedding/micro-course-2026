## Principal-Agent Problems

Moral hazard occurs when one party (agent) can take hidden actions that affect another (principal) after a contract is signed. Classic examples:

- Employer (principal) cannot observe worker (agent) effort
- Insurer (principal) cannot monitor insured (agent) risk-taking

In Australia:

- Workplace productivity contracts
- Financial advisor incentives
- Insurance deductibles

The principal's problem is to design a contract that:

1. Satisfies participation constraint (agent willing to work)
2. Satisfies incentive compatibility constraint (agent prefers working to shirking)

Mathematically, maximize principal's expected profit subject to:

PC: E[u(w(e)) - c(e)] ≥ ū
IC: e ∈ argmax E[u(w(e)) - c(e)]

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Consider an Australian construction company (principal) hiring a subcontractor (agent):

- High effort (e=1) costs $200 but ensures on-time completion
- Low effort (e=0) costs $0 but risks delays (50% chance of $1000 penalty)

Optimal contract must satisfy:

PC: w - 200 ≥ 0
IC: w - 200 ≥ 0.5w + 0.5(w - 1000)

Solving yields w ≥ $1200 for high effort to be optimal.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Moral hazard means the agent is being immoral"

Correction: Moral hazard describes incentive misalignment, not moral judgment. It's about observable vs unobservable actions, not ethics.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Hidden actions create moral hazard
- Contracts must balance participation and incentives
- Efficiency wages may be necessary
- Performance-based pay aligns incentives
