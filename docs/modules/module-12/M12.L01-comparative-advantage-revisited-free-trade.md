## Learning Objective

Explain how comparative advantage drives gains from international trade.

---

## The Foundation of Trade

Comparative advantage, first articulated by David Ricardo in 1817, remains the bedrock justification for free trade. It states that countries should specialize in producing goods where they have the lowest opportunity cost, then trade for other goods. Even if one nation is absolutely more efficient at producing everything (absolute advantage), trade still benefits both parties when based on comparative advantage.

Australia exemplifies this principle through its exports of iron ore, LNG, and agricultural products. While Australia could produce manufactured goods, its opportunity cost is lower in resource extraction due to abundant natural endowments. Specializing in these sectors and trading for manufactured goods expands Australia's consumption possibilities beyond what its domestic production alone could achieve.

---

## Worked Example

**Australia's Iron Ore Trade with China**

1. Suppose Australia produces either 100 tons of iron ore or 50 cars per year
2. China produces either 80 tons of iron ore or 100 cars per year
3. Australia's opportunity cost: 1 car = 2 tons iron ore
4. China's opportunity cost: 1 car = 0.8 tons iron ore
5. Terms of trade must be between these ratios (e.g., 1 car = 1.5 tons)
6. Both nations gain by specializing and trading at intermediate terms

---

## Common Misconception

> "Countries should only export goods they're best at producing"

This confuses absolute advantage (being best) with comparative advantage (lowest opportunity cost). Trade benefits occur when nations specialize based on relative, not absolute, efficiencies. Australia exports education services not because it's the "best" but because its opportunity cost is favorable compared to alternatives.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Trade creates gains when based on comparative (not absolute) advantage
- Specialization expands total production possibilities
- Terms of trade must lie between nations' domestic opportunity costs
- Australia's comparative advantages include resource extraction and education
