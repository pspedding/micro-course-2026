## Learning Objective

Describe Australia's major trading relationships and the role of free trade agreements.

---

## Australia's Trade Structure

Australia is a highly trade-exposed economy — trade (exports + imports) represents roughly 45% of GDP. The structure is distinctive:

**Top exports (goods):**
- Iron ore (~$130bn/yr) — overwhelmingly to China
- LNG (liquefied natural gas) — Japan, China, South Korea
- Coal (metallurgical and thermal)
- Gold, beef, wheat, wool

**Top service exports:**
- Education (~$35bn pre-COVID) — largest service export
- Tourism/travel

**Top trading partners:** China (by far the largest, ~30% of exports), Japan, South Korea, USA, India, UK

Australia runs a trade surplus in goods (driven by resources) but a deficit in services (tourism, education imports exceed exports in some periods).

---

## Free Trade Agreements

Australia has pursued bilateral and regional FTAs extensively:

| Agreement | Partners | In force |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| AUSFTA | USA | 2005 |
| SAFTA | Singapore | 2003 |
| TAFTA | Thailand | 2005 |
| AANZFTA | ASEAN + NZ | 2010 |
| ChAFTA | China | 2015 |
| KAFTA | Korea | 2014 |
| JAEPA | Japan | 2015 |
| CPTPP | 11 countries incl. Japan, Canada, Mexico | 2018 |
| AIFTA | India | 2022 |
| AUKESFTA | UK | 2023 |

FTAs reduce tariffs and sometimes non-tariff barriers (like quarantine standards), increase market access, and lock in trade liberalisation reforms.

---

## Trade Diversification Challenge

Australia's heavy dependence on China creates vulnerability. In 2020–21, China imposed trade restrictions on Australian barley (80.5% tariff), wine, beef, coal, cotton, and lobster — largely as political retaliation over Australian calls for a COVID-19 inquiry. Australian exporters lost ~$20bn in market access.

Australia responded by successfully diversifying some exports (coal to Japan/Korea/India; barley to Saudi Arabia) while pursuing WTO dispute resolution. By 2023, most restrictions were lifted. The episode highlighted the risk of trade concentration.

---

## Worked Example

**ChAFTA and Australian Beef**

Before ChAFTA, Chinese tariffs on Australian beef were 12–25%. ChAFTA phased these to zero by 2024. Australian beef exports to China grew from ~$600m (2014) to over $3bn (2019). Consumer prices in China fell; Australian cattle farmers expanded production.

This illustrates comparative advantage in action: Australia's vast land, efficient ranching, and food safety reputation give it genuine comparative advantage in beef production.

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Australia should be self-sufficient and reduce dependence on imports."**

Pursuing self-sufficiency means producing goods at high opportunity cost domestically rather than buying them cheaply overseas. This reduces living standards. The goal of trade policy is not self-sufficiency but *diversification of trading partners* to manage the risk of any single partner disrupting trade.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Australia is resource-rich and export-dependent; China is the dominant trading partner (~30% of exports)
- FTAs have expanded market access and locked in tariff reductions across Asia-Pacific
- Trade concentration creates vulnerability — the 2020 China trade restrictions demonstrated this clearly
- Australia's policy response was to diversify export markets while pursuing WTO dispute resolution

## Practice

1. Australia exports iron ore worth $130bn per year, mostly to China. What does this reveal about Australia's comparative advantage? What are the risks of this concentration?
2. ChAFTA reduced Chinese tariffs on Australian beef to zero. Using supply-demand analysis, show who benefits and who loses in Australia and China.
3. Why might Australia join the CPTPP even if it already has bilateral FTAs with several CPTPP members?
