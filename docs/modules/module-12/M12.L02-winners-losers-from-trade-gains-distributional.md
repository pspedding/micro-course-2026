## Learning Objective

Identify the distributional effects of international trade on consumers and producers.

---

## Gains from Trade: The Aggregate Picture

When Australia opens to international trade in a good, the world price determines the domestic price. If the world price is *below* the pre-trade domestic price, Australia imports — domestic consumers gain from lower prices while domestic producers face increased competition.

Using supply-and-demand analysis:
- **Consumer surplus rises** (area under demand, above the lower world price)
- **Producer surplus falls** (area above supply, below the lower world price)
- **Net gain**: the gain to consumers exceeds the loss to producers — total economic surplus increases

This net gain is the fundamental economic case for free trade. The gains are real and measurable; Australia's access to cheaper manufactured goods, electronics, and clothing has raised household living standards significantly since tariff reductions in the 1980s–2000s.

---

## Winners and Losers

Despite aggregate gains, trade creates clear distributional effects:

**Winners from imports:**
- Consumers (lower prices, greater variety)
- Downstream industries using imported inputs (e.g. manufacturers using imported steel)
- Export industries (partner countries earn income to buy Australian exports)

**Losers from imports:**
- Import-competing domestic producers (e.g. Australian textile manufacturers, car makers)
- Workers in those industries (job losses, wage pressure)

Australia's automotive industry is a stark example: Holden, Ford, and Toyota all ceased Australian manufacturing by 2017, partly due to import competition. Workers displaced faced real adjustment costs.

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Steel and Manufacturing**

Suppose the pre-trade domestic price of steel is $900/tonne and the world price is $600/tonne.

- At $600, Australian consumers (manufacturers) buy more steel — consumer surplus rises
- Australian steel producers lose market share — producer surplus falls
- Net welfare calculation: if consumer gain = $2.1bn and producer loss = $1.4bn, net gain = $700m

The $700m net gain is real, but the $1.4bn loss is concentrated in steel workers and shareholders while the $2.1bn gain is spread across all steel-using industries.

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Free trade always makes everyone better off."**

Free trade increases *total* economic surplus but creates winners and losers. Import-competing workers and firms can experience genuine hardship. The correct statement is that the gains are large enough to *potentially* compensate the losers — but compensation rarely happens fully in practice.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Trade raises total economic surplus but redistributes income from import-competing industries to consumers
- Consumer gains from imports exceed producer losses in aggregate
- Adjustment costs for displaced workers are real and politically significant
- The compensation principle says gainers could compensate losers, but this requires active policy

## Practice

1. Australia opens to imported clothing at a world price below the domestic price. Using a supply-demand diagram, show the change in consumer surplus, producer surplus, and total surplus.
2. Why might a worker in Australia's former car manufacturing industry oppose free trade even if aggregate gains are positive?
3. If the world price of a good is *above* Australia's domestic price, who gains and who loses when Australia opens to trade?
