## Learning Objective

Identify why long-run average costs change with scale.

---

## Long-Run Cost Concepts

Australian firms experience:
- Economies of Scale: LRATC falls as output increases
  - Reasons: Specialization (BHP's mining engineers), efficient capital (Woolworths distribution), technology (CSL's vaccine production)
- Constant Returns: LRATC constant
- Diseconomies: LRATC rises (CBA's bureaucratic delays)

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES) is smallest output where LRATC is minimized. The LRATC curve is the "envelope" of all possible SRATC curves.

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Airline Expansion**

1. Small (Rex Airlines):
   - High LRATC ($0.50/seat-km)
   - Can't negotiate bulk fuel discounts
2. Medium (Virgin):
   - LRATC = $0.35 (economies from route optimization)
3. Large (Qantas):
   - LRATC = $0.30 (global maintenance hubs)
4. Oversized:
   - LRATC = $0.33 (diseconomies from complex logistics)

---

## Common Misconception

> "Bigger companies always have lower costs."

Actually, beyond optimal scale (like some Australian banks), coordination problems and bureaucracy can increase average costs.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Economies: LRATC falls with scale
- Diseconomies: LRATC rises beyond optimal scale
- MES is minimum efficient scale
- LRATC envelopes SRATC curves
