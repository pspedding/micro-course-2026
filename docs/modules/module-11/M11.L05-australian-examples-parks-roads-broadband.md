## Learning Objective

Apply goods classification to Australian infrastructure debates.

---

## Case Studies

**National Parks**
- Club good aspects: Entry fees fund maintenance (excludable)
- Public good arguments: Conservation benefits all (non-excludable)

**Road Pricing**
- Toll roads (WestConnex): Excludable via e-Tags
- Congestion charging: Proposed for Sydney CBD to manage demand

**NBN Broadband**
- Near-public good: Universal service obligation
- Private provision: Retail competition over public infrastructure

---

## Worked Example

**Assessing Sydney Harbour Bridge**
1. Without toll: Common resource (rivalrous in peak, non-excludable)
2. With toll: Club good (excludable, but still rivalrous at capacity)
3. Dynamic pricing: Higher tolls in peak periods manage rivalry

---

## Common Misconception

> "User-pays systems are always more efficient"

While user-pays can improve allocation (e.g., toll roads), equity concerns arise for essential services. Australia's mixed approach balances efficiency and access.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Many goods have mixed characteristics
- Pricing mechanisms can alter good classification
- Australian examples show policy tradeoffs
- No one-size-fits-all solution
