## Learning Objective

Explain why markets underprovide public goods due to the free-rider problem.

---

## The Free-Rider Problem

Public goods face underprovision because people can benefit without paying (free-riding). Since providers can't exclude non-payers, private firms lack profit incentive. 

Australian examples:
- **ABC broadcasting**: Listeners benefit even if they don't pay license fees
- **Street lighting**: All residents benefit regardless of tax contributions
- **National defense**: Protection extends to all citizens

Demand for public goods sums vertically (willingness-to-pay stacked) rather than horizontally as with private goods.

---

## Worked Example

**Calculating Optimal Public Good Provision**
1. Three residents value additional streetlights differently:
   - Resident A: $150
   - Resident B: $100
   - Resident C: $50
2. New light costs $250 to install
3. Sum of valuations ($300) > Cost → efficient to provide
4. Market failure occurs if no mechanism exists to collect payments

---

## Common Misconception

> "Free-riders are just being selfish"

Free-riding is rational individual behavior - why pay if you'll benefit anyway? The problem is structural, not moral. Solutions require collective action mechanisms.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Free-riders enjoy benefits without contributing
- Markets underprovide non-excludable goods
- Optimal quantity occurs where ∑MWP = MC
- Government provision often necessary
