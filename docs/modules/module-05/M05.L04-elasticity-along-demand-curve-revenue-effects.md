## Learning Objective

Explain how elasticity varies along a linear demand curve and its impact on total revenue.

---

## Elasticity Along the Demand Curve

For a linear demand curve:
- **Top:** Elastic (PED >1), where small price changes cause large quantity changes.
- **Midpoint:** Unit elastic (PED =1), where total revenue (TR = P × Q) is maximised.
- **Bottom:** Inelastic (PED <1), where price changes have minimal effect on quantity.

Australian example: Electricity demand is inelastic at low prices but becomes elastic as prices rise.

---

## Worked Example

**Revenue Effects for Sydney Coffee Shops**

1. **Demand Curve:** \( P = 10 - Q \).
2. **Midpoint:** At P=$5, Q=5 (unit elastic).
3. **TR Test:**
   - **Price ↑ $6 → Q=4:** TR = $24 (was $25 at P=$5).
   - **Price ↓ $4 → Q=6:** TR = $24 (again, less than $25).

---

## Common Misconception

> "Lowering prices always increases revenue."

Only true in elastic regions; in inelastic regions, price cuts reduce revenue.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Elasticity varies along linear demand curves.
- TR is maximised at unit elasticity.
- The "total revenue test" predicts TR changes from price moves.
- Australian firms use this for pricing (e.g., airlines, utilities).
