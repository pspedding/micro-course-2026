## Learning Objective

Calculate and interpret price elasticity of demand (PED) using the midpoint method.

---

## Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

PED measures how quantity demanded responds to price changes: \( PED = \frac{\%\Delta Q_d}{\%\Delta P} \). Its absolute value determines elasticity:
- **Elastic (>1):** Quantity changes more than price (e.g., luxury goods).
- **Inelastic (<1):** Quantity changes less than price (e.g., necessities).
- **Unit elastic (=1):** Proportional changes.

Australian examples include fuel (inelastic) and airline tickets (elastic).

---

## Worked Example

**Calculating PED for Cigarettes in Australia**

1. **Initial Price (P1):** $30/pack, Quantity (Q1): 1,000 packs.
2. **New Price (P2):** $33/pack, Quantity (Q2): 900 packs.
3. **Midpoint Method:** 
   - \( \%\Delta Q = \frac{900-1000}{(900+1000)/2} \times 100 = -10.53\% \)
   - \( \%\Delta P = \frac{33-30}{(33+30)/2} \times 100 = 9.52\% \)
   - \( PED = \frac{-10.53}{9.52} \approx -1.11 \) (elastic).

---

## Common Misconception

> "Elasticity is the same as slope."

Slope measures absolute changes, while elasticity measures percentage changes, which vary along a linear demand curve.

---

## Key Takeaways

- PED = %ΔQd / %ΔP (midpoint method avoids direction bias).
- Elasticity depends on substitutes, necessity, income proportion, and time.
- Australian tobacco taxes exploit inelastic demand.
- Perfectly elastic/inelastic curves are theoretical extremes.
