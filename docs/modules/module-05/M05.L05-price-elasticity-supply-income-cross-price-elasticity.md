## Learning Objective

Calculate price elasticity of supply (PES), income elasticity (YED), and cross-price elasticity (XED) for Australian markets.

---

## Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

PES measures how quantity supplied responds to price changes: \( PES = \frac{\%\Delta Q_s}{\%\Delta P} \).
- **Time horizons:** Short-run PES is low (fixed capacity); long-run PES is higher (adjustable inputs).

## Income Elasticity (YED)

YED measures how demand changes with income: \( YED = \frac{\%\Delta Q_d}{\%\Delta Y} \).
- **Normal goods:** YED >0 (e.g., cars).
- **Inferior goods:** YED <0 (e.g., instant noodles).

## Cross-Price Elasticity (XED)

XED measures how demand for Good A responds to price changes in Good B: \( XED = \frac{\%\Delta Q_{dA}}{\%\Delta P_B} \).
- **Substitutes:** XED >0 (e.g., butter vs. margarine).
- **Complements:** XED <0 (e.g., petrol vs. cars).

Australian example: Housing supply elasticity varies across cities (Sydney vs. Adelaide).

---

## Worked Example

**Calculating PES for Australian Wheat**

1. **Initial Price (P1):** $200/tonne, Quantity (Q1): 1,000 tonnes.
2. **New Price (P2):** $220/tonne, Quantity (Q2): 1,100 tonnes.
3. **PES:** \( \frac{(1100-1000)/1050}{(220-200)/210} \times 100 = 1.0 \) (unit elastic).

---

## Common Misconception

> "All goods have positive income elasticity."

Inferior goods (e.g., used cars) have negative YED as demand falls with rising income.

---

## Key Takeaways

- PES depends on time horizon and input flexibility.
- YED classifies goods as normal or inferior.
- XED identifies substitutes and complements.
- Australian housing markets show low short-run PES.

---

## Practice

1. If PES = 0.5 and price rises 10%, what happens to Qs?
2. Calculate XED if the price of tea rises 5% and coffee demand rises 3%.
3. Why is Sydney housing supply inelastic?
