## Learning Objective

Calculate producer surplus and total economic surplus, and explain their role in market efficiency.

---

## Producer Surplus (PS) and Total Surplus (TS)

Producer surplus is the difference between the price received by sellers and their minimum acceptable price (cost). Graphically, it is the area above the supply curve but below the price line. Total economic surplus (TS) is the sum of consumer surplus (CS) and producer surplus (PS), representing the total net benefit to society from a market transaction.

In Australia, PS can be observed in agricultural markets, where farmers receive prices above their production costs during high-demand seasons. TS maximisation indicates an efficient allocation of resources.

---

## Worked Example

**Calculating PS and TS for Avocado Farmers**

1. **Supply Curve:** Suppose the supply curve for avocados is \( P = 2 + 0.5Q \), where P is in AUD/kg.
2. **Market Price:** Avocados sell at $6/kg.
3. **Equilibrium Quantity:** \( 6 = 2 + 0.5Q \) → \( Q = 8 \).
4. **PS Calculation:** Area of the triangle = \( \frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times (6-2) \) = $16.
5. **TS Calculation:** If CS is $24 (from demand side), then TS = $24 + $16 = $40.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Higher prices always increase producer surplus."

While higher prices can increase PS, they may reduce CS and overall TS if demand is elastic, leading to deadweight loss.

---

## Key Takeaways

- PS = Price minus cost, represented as the area above the supply curve below the price.
- TS = CS + PS; markets maximise TS when supply equals demand.
- Deadweight loss occurs when TS is not maximised (e.g., taxes, price controls).
- Australian examples include agricultural markets and housing.
