## Learning Objective

Calculate consumer surplus using willingness to pay and demand curves.

---

## Willingness to Pay (WTP)

WTP is the maximum price a consumer would pay for a good. In Australia, consider toll roads: drivers value time savings differently, creating a range of WTP (e.g., $5–$20 for a Sydney tunnel).

**Consumer Surplus:** The difference between WTP and the actual price paid. For a $10 toll, a driver with $15 WTP gains $5 surplus.

---

## Worked Example

**Sydney Harbour Bridge Toll**

1. Drivers' WTP: $8, $10, $12, $15.
2. Toll price: $10.
3. Surplus: $0, $0, $2, $5 (total surplus = $7).
4. Graphically, this is the area above $10 and below the demand curve.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Consumer surplus is the same as profit."

Profit is revenue minus costs (producer-side), while surplus measures consumer benefit.

---

## Key Takeaways

- WTP varies by individual and context.
- Consumer surplus quantifies net consumer benefit.
- Surplus is area under demand curve and above price.
- Policies (e.g., subsidies) can alter surplus.
