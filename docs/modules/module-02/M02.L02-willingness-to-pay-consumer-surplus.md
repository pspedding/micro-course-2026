## Learning Objective

Calculate consumer surplus using willingness to pay and demand curves.

---

## Willingness to Pay (WTP)

WTP is the maximum price a consumer would pay for a good. In Australia, consider toll roads: drivers value time savings differently, creating a range of WTP (e.g., $5–$20 for a Sydney tunnel).

**Consumer Surplus:** The difference between WTP and the actual price paid. For a $10 toll, a driver with $15 WTP gains $5 surplus.

---

## Worked Example

**Sydney Harbour Bridge Toll**

1. Drivers' WTP: $8, $10, $12, $15.
2. Toll price: $10.
3. Surplus: $0, $0, $2, $5 (total surplus = $7).
4. Graphically, this is the area above $10 and below the demand curve.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Consumer surplus is the same as profit."

Profit is revenue minus costs (producer-side), while surplus measures consumer benefit.

---

## Key Takeaways

- WTP varies by individual and context.
- Consumer surplus quantifies net consumer benefit.
- Surplus is area under demand curve and above price.
- Policies (e.g., subsidies) can alter surplus.

---

## Exam-Style Calculation Practice

!!! tip "Key technique: MB curve = demand curve = WTP for each unit"
    The marginal benefit (MB) curve shows the **maximum price** a consumer will pay for each successive unit. To find WTP for unit *n*, substitute q = n into the MB equation.

**Example 1:** MB curve is **MB = 150 − 2q**.
- WTP for unit 1: MB = 150 − 2(1) = **$148**
- WTP for unit 10: MB = 150 − 2(10) = **$130**
- At what quantity does WTP = $0? Set 150 − 2q = 0 → q = 75

**Example 2:** Market price = $90, MB = 150 − 2q.
- Quantity purchased: set MB = P → 150 − 2q = 90 → **q* = 30**
- Consumer surplus = ½ × base × height = ½ × 30 × (150 − 90) = **$900**

## Practice

1. A consumer's MB = 120 − 3q. What is the maximum price they will pay for unit 15? At what quantity does demand reach zero?
2. Calculate surplus if WTPs are $20, $18, $15, $12 and price is $16.
3. If MB = 80 − q and price = $40, find (a) quantity purchased and (b) total consumer surplus.
