## Understanding the Demand Curve

The demand curve is a graphical representation of the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded, holding all else constant. In Australia, consider the demand for avocados: as prices rise during supply shortages (e.g., due to weather disruptions), fewer consumers purchase them, illustrating a downward-sloping demand curve.

Key concepts:
- **Law of Demand:** Higher prices lead to lower quantities demanded (and vice versa).
- **Demand Schedule:** Table of prices and corresponding quantities demanded.
- **Real Income Effect:** As prices rise, purchasing power decreases, reducing demand.
- **Substitution Effect:** Consumers switch to alternatives (e.g., switching from avocados to bananas).
- **Diminishing Marginal Utility:** Each additional unit consumed provides less satisfaction.

---

## Worked Example

**Avocado Demand in Sydney**

1. At $3/avocado, demand is 1,000 units/week.
2. At $5/avocado, demand drops to 600 units/week.
3. Plotting these points reveals a downward-sloping curve.

---

## Common Misconception

> "A demand curve shift is the same as movement along the curve."

A shift occurs when a non-price factor (e.g., income) changes demand at all prices, while movement along the curve reflects price changes.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The demand curve slopes downward due to the law of demand.
- Real income and substitution effects explain consumer responses to price changes.
- Diminishing marginal utility underlies the curve's shape.
- Shifts (vs. movements) are critical for accurate analysis.
