## Learning Objective

Define pure monopoly and identify the major barriers to entry that create monopolies.

---

## Understanding Monopoly Power

A pure monopoly exists when a single firm is the sole producer of a product with no close substitutes, giving it significant market power to set prices. Unlike competitive markets where firms are price takers, monopolists are price makers. In Australia, examples include the National Broadband Network (NBN) for fixed-line broadband infrastructure and state-owned water utilities.

Key barriers to entry that create and maintain monopolies include:

1. **Natural monopoly**: When economies of scale are so large that one firm can supply the entire market at lower cost than multiple firms (e.g., electricity grids, NBN)
2. **Government regulation/licenses**: Legal barriers like patents (20 years for pharmaceuticals) or operating licenses (Australia Post's reserved postal service)
3. **Control of key resources**: Exclusive access to essential inputs (e.g., mining rights)
4. **Network effects**: When a product's value increases with more users (e.g., social media platforms)

---

## Worked Example

**Australia Post's Reserved Postal Service**

1. Identify the market: Standard letter delivery in Australia
2. Recognize the legal barrier: The Competition and Consumer Act 2010 grants Australia Post exclusive rights to deliver letters under 250g
3. Understand the economic rationale: Maintaining universal service obligations
4. Consider alternatives: Courier services can operate for parcels but not basic letters

---

## Common Misconception

> "Monopolies always earn huge profits"

While monopolies have pricing power, they may not always earn excessive profits. Natural monopolies like water utilities often have regulated prices, and some monopolies face high fixed costs (e.g., NBN infrastructure). Profit depends on the relationship between price, costs, and quantity sold.

---

## Key Takeaways

- A pure monopoly has a single seller with no close substitutes
- Barriers to entry prevent competition and sustain monopoly power
- Australian examples include regulated utilities and government-granted monopolies
- Not all monopolies earn excessive profits due to regulation or high costs
