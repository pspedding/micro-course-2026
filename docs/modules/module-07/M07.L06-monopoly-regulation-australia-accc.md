## Learning Objective

Describe the ACCC's role in regulating monopolies and promoting competition.

---

## The ACCC's Powers and Tools

The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) regulates monopolies through:

1. **Price Surveillance**: Monitoring utilities like electricity and water
2. **Access Declarations**: Forcing infrastructure sharing (e.g., NBN, gas pipelines)
3. **Anti-competitive Conduct Laws**: Preventing abuse of market power (Section 46)
4. **Merger Control**: Blocking acquisitions that substantially lessen competition

Key legislation: Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (CCA)

---

## Worked Example

**Regulating Natural Monopolies: Electricity Networks**

1. Identify natural monopoly characteristics: High fixed costs, economies of scale
2. Apply regulatory tools:
   - Price caps (CPI-X framework)
   - Performance standards (reliability metrics)
   - Ring-fencing (separating competitive/generation from regulated/transmission)
3. Recent cases: 2023 default market offer decisions limiting price rises

---

## Common Misconception

> "The ACCC sets prices for all monopolies"

The ACCC doesn't directly set most prices - it establishes frameworks (like price caps) and intervenes in specific cases. Many prices are still determined by markets within regulated constraints.

---

## Key Takeaways

- ACCC balances efficiency and fairness in monopoly regulation
- Natural monopolies are price-controlled rather than broken up
- Access regimes promote competition in dependent markets
- Recent focus areas: digital platforms, supermarkets, airlines
- Penalties for anti-competitive conduct can reach 10% of turnover
