## Learning Objective

Derive individual demand functions from utility maximisation problems.

---

## Demand Function Derivation

From the utility maximisation solution, we obtain demand functions x₁*(p₁,p₂,m). For Cobb-Douglas utility U = x₁ᵃx₂ᵇ:

x₁* = (a/(a+b))·(m/p₁)

Key properties:
1. Homogeneous of degree 0: x₁*(tp₁,tp₂,tm) = x₁*(p₁,p₂,m)
2. Downward sloping in own-price (law of demand)
3. Engel curve shows x₁* vs m

Australian example: The demand for avocado toast (x₁) follows x₁* = 0.6m/p₁ in Sydney cafes.

---

## Worked Example

**Melbourne Coffee Demand**

Suppose utility over coffee (x₁) and tea (x₂) is U = x₁⁰·⁷⁵x₂⁰·²⁵ with m = $50:

1. Demand functions:
   x₁* = (0.75/1)·(50/p₁) = 37.5/p₁
   x₂* = (0.25/1)·(50/p₂) = 12.5/p₂
2. At p₁ = $4.50 (typical Melbourne flat white):
   x₁* ≈ 8.33 coffees/week
3. Demand curve: x₁* = 37.5/p₁ (rectangular hyperbola)

---

## Common Misconception

> "Demand curves are linear in practice"

Most Australian demand curves (housing, education, healthcare) show non-linear price sensitivity, especially at income thresholds.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Demand functions emerge from utility maximisation solutions
- Cobb-Douglas yields constant budget shares
- Homogeneity means demand responds to real (not nominal) values
- Price-consumption curve traces optimal bundles as price changes
- Australian examples include: petrol demand, NBN internet plans
