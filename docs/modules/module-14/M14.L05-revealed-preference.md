## Learning Objective

Test consumer consistency using revealed preference theory.

---

## Revealed Preference Concepts

**Directly Revealed Preferred (DRP):** Bundle A is DRP to B if A chosen when B was affordable

**Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference (WARP):** If A DRP to B, then B cannot be DRP to A under different prices/budget

Australian application: Test whether household energy choices (2019 vs 2023) satisfy WARP despite price shocks.

---

## Worked Example

**Adelaide Grocery Choices**

Observation 1 (Jan): p₁=$4 (milk), p₂=$5 (juice), chosen bundle A=(2,3)
Observation 2 (Jul): p₁'=$5, p₂'=$4, chosen bundle B=(3,2)

Check WARP:
1. Cost of A at Jan prices: 4×2 + 5×3 = $23
   Cost of B at Jan prices: 4×3 + 5×2 = $22 → A was feasible when B chosen
2. Cost of B at Jul prices: 5×3 + 4×2 = $23
   Cost of A at Jul prices: 5×2 + 4×3 = $22 → B was feasible when A chosen
3. Violation: Both A DRP to B and B DRP to A

---

## Common Misconception

> "WARP violations mean irrationality"

Australian consumers may rationally violate WARP due to:
- Changing preferences (seasonal produce)
- Quality adjustments (smaller "shrinkflation" packages)
- New information (health studies)

---

## Key Takeaways

- Choices reveal preferences without utility functions
- WARP tests consistency across budget scenarios
- Index numbers (Laspeyres/Paasche) measure welfare changes
- Australian CPI uses revealed preference logic
- Policy applications: NBN adoption, Medicare utilization
