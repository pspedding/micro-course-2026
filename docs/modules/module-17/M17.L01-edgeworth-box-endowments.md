## Learning Objective

Construct and interpret the Edgeworth box diagram for a two-consumer, two-good exchange economy.

---

## The Edgeworth Box Framework

The Edgeworth box is a graphical representation of an exchange economy with two consumers (A and B) and two goods (X and Y). The box's dimensions represent the total quantities of goods X (width) and Y (height) in the economy. Any point in the box represents an allocation where:

- Consumer A gets (x_A, y_A)
- Consumer B gets (x_B, y_B) = (X_total - x_A, Y_total - y_A)

The endowment point (ω₁, ω₂) shows the initial allocation before any trade occurs. For example, in an Australian context, consider two households:

- Household A starts with 80% of food but only 20% of clothing
- Household B has the remaining 20% of food and 80% of clothing

Mathematically, if total endowments are X̄ and Ȳ, then:

x_A + x_B = X̄
y_A + y_B = Ȳ

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Consider two Australian farmers trading water rights (X) and livestock (Y), with total endowments X̄ = 1000 ML, Ȳ = 500 head. Farmer A starts with ω_A = (800 ML, 100 head), Farmer B with ω_B = (200 ML, 400 head).

1. Draw the Edgeworth box (1000 units wide, 500 tall)
2. Plot ω at (800,100)
3. Show indifference curves (Cobb-Douglas utility: U_A = x_A^0.6 y_A^0.4)
4. The lens-shaped area between the indifference curves represents mutually beneficial trades

---

## Common Misconception

> "The endowment point must be at the center of the box"

Correction: The endowment can be anywhere in the box; its position reflects initial inequality in resource distribution, like Australia's uneven water allocations.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The Edgeworth box visually represents all possible allocations in a two-good, two-consumer economy
- The endowment point shows initial resource distribution before trade
- The lens-shaped area between indifference curves shows potential Pareto improvements
- Australian examples include water rights trading and emissions permit markets
