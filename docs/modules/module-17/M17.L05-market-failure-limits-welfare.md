## Learning Objective

Identify situations where competitive markets fail to achieve Pareto efficiency.

---

## When Markets Fail

The First Welfare Theorem's assumptions often fail in reality:

1. **Externalities**: Unpriced side effects (e.g., Australia's coal seam gas water contamination)
   - Social cost > private cost → overproduction
   - Solution: Pigovian taxes (Australia's carbon pricing)

2. **Public Goods**: Non-excludable, non-rival (e.g., national defense, clean air)
   - Free rider problem → underprovision
   - Australian example: Public broadcasting (ABC)

3. **Market Power**: Monopolies distort prices (e.g., Australia's banking oligopoly)
   - MR ≠ P → deadweight loss
   - Remedy: ACCC regulation

Mathematically, externalities create divergence between private and social MRS.

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Coal power externality:
1. Private MC = $30/MWh
2. Social MC = $50/MWh (includes health costs)
3. Competitive equilibrium: Q_market where P = $30
4. Efficient quantity: Q* where P = $50
5. Deadweight loss = ∫_{Q*}^{Q_market} (50 - 30) dQ

Policy options:
- Carbon tax of $20/MWh
- Cap-and-trade (Australia's Safeguard Mechanism)

---

## Common Misconception

> "Government intervention always improves efficiency"

Correction: Government failure can worsen outcomes (e.g., Australia's home insulation program).

---

## Key Takeaways

- Market failures are pervasive in real economies
- Australian policy provides concrete examples of each failure type
- The Second Theorem suggests redistribution + markets can address equity
- Policy design must account for potential government failures
