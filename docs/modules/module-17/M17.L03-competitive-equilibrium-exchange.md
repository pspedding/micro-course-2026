## Learning Objective

Determine competitive equilibrium prices and allocations in a pure exchange economy.

---

## Walrasian Equilibrium

In a competitive exchange economy:

1. Consumers take prices (p_x, p_y) as given
2. Markets clear when supply = demand for both goods
3. Walras' Law: If n-1 markets clear, the nth must also clear
4. Equilibrium allocation is Pareto efficient (First Welfare Theorem)

Australian context: The Emissions Reduction Fund creates a market where p_x = price per ton CO₂, with equilibrium emerging from bid/ask spreads.

Mathematically, solve:

1. Consumer A's problem: max U_A(x_A,y_A) s.t. p_x x_A + p_y y_A = p_x ω_xA + p_y ω_yA
2. Consumer B's problem: similarly
3. Market clearing: x_A + x_B = X̄, y_A + y_B = Ȳ

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Two regions trade renewable energy (X) and coal (Y):
- U_A = x_A^0.7 y_A^0.3, ω_A = (100,50)
- U_B = x_B^0.3 y_B^0.7, ω_B = (50,100)
- Total endowments: X̄ = 150, Ȳ = 150

1. Set p_y = 1 (numeraire), find p_x
2. Solve demand functions:
   x_A = 0.7*(100p_x + 50)/p_x
   y_A = 0.3*(100p_x + 50)
3. Market clearing: x_A + x_B = 150 → p_x = 1
4. Equilibrium allocation: x_A = 105, y_A = 45, x_B = 45, y_B = 105

---

## Common Misconception

> "Equilibrium prices are always positive"

Correction: Free goods can have zero price (e.g., Australia's solar surplus periods when p_x ≈ 0).

---

## Key Takeaways

- Competitive equilibrium requires market clearing and utility maximization
- Walras' Law simplifies finding equilibrium prices
- Numeraire normalization reduces degrees of freedom
- Australian energy markets show real-world price discovery
