## Learning Objective

Explain the conditions under which competitive markets achieve Pareto efficiency.

---

## The Fundamental Welfare Theorems

1. **First Welfare Theorem**: Any competitive equilibrium is Pareto efficient
   - Assumes: no externalities, perfect information, complete markets
   - Australian example: National Electricity Market achieves efficiency under normal conditions

2. **Second Welfare Theorem**: Any efficient allocation can be decentralized via competitive markets after appropriate lump-sum transfers
   - Requires: convex preferences, no wealth effects
   - Australian application: Carbon tax revenue redistribution

Mathematically, the theorems rely on:
- Separation of efficiency and equity
- Convexity of preference sets
- Price-taking behavior

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Consider two welfare states:
1. Initial unequal endowments lead to equilibrium at point E1
2. Social planner prefers more equitable allocation E2 on contract curve
3. Implement lump-sum transfer from rich to poor (e.g., Australia's GST redistribution)
4. New equilibrium E2 is both efficient and equitable

Calculus: For U_A = x_A^α y_A^(1-α), U_B = x_B^β y_B^(1-β), the transfer must satisfy:

(p_x Δx + p_y Δy)_A = -(p_x Δx + p_y Δy)_B

## Common Misconception

> "The Second Theorem means we can always redistribute without efficiency costs"

Correction: Real-world taxes distort behavior (e.g., Australia's debate over dividend imputation).

---

## Key Takeaways

- First Theorem: markets → efficiency under ideal conditions
- Second Theorem: redistribution + markets → any desired efficient outcome
- Australian policy debates often center on these theorems' limitations
- Practical applications require careful attention to assumptions
