## Pareto Efficiency Defined

An allocation is Pareto efficient if no reallocation can make one consumer better off without making the other worse off. In the Edgeworth box:

1. Pareto improvements move northeast/southwest within the lens-shaped area
2. Efficiency occurs where indifference curves are tangent (MRS_A = MRS_B)
3. The contract curve connects all such efficient points

Australian example: The National Electricity Market achieves Pareto efficiency when state energy trades balance marginal benefits.

Mathematically, for utility functions U_A(x_A,y_A) and U_B(x_B,y_B):

‚ąāU_A/‚ąāx_A / ‚ąāU_A/‚ąāy_A = ‚ąāU_B/‚ąāx_B / ‚ąāU_B/‚ąāy_B

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Two Australian states trade electricity (X) and gas (Y) with:
- U_A = ln(x_A) + 2ln(y_A)
- U_B = 2ln(x_B) + ln(y_B)
- Total endowments: XŐĄ = 3000 GWh, »≤ = 2000 TJ

1. Calculate MRS for both states:
   MRS_A = y_A/(2x_A)
   MRS_B = (2y_B)/x_B
2. Set MRS_A = MRS_B and use x_B = 3000 - x_A, y_B = 2000 - y_A
3. Solve to find contract curve: y_A = (4000x_A)/(3000 + x_A)

## Common Misconception

> "Any point on the contract curve is equally fair"

Correction: While all points are efficient, they represent different distributions of welfare. Australia's energy market rules explicitly consider equity alongside efficiency.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Pareto efficiency requires MRS_A = MRS_B
- The contract curve contains all efficient allocations
- Efficiency ‚Č† fairness - Australia's carbon policy debates highlight this distinction
- Core allocations are those where no coalition can do better on their own
