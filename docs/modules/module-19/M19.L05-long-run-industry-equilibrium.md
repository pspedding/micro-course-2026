## Long-Run Industry Equilibrium and Zero Profit

In the **long-run**, free entry/exit drives economic profit to zero:
- Firms produce at minimum long-run average total cost (\(P = \min LRATC\)).
- The **efficient scale** is the output where \(LRATC\) is minimized.
- Industry size adjusts until \(P = LRATC\) (zero economic profit).

**Rent and quasi-rent**:
- **Economic rent**: Payments to inputs above opportunity cost.
- **Quasi-rent**: Short-run returns to fixed inputs (e.g., machinery).

**Zero economic profit ≠ zero accounting profit**: Accountants exclude opportunity costs (e.g., owner's time).

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Australian cafes face \(LRATC = y^2 - 10y + 30\). Market demand is \(D(p) = 200 - 2p\).

1. **Efficient scale**: Minimize \(LRATC\): \(\frac{d}{dy} LRATC = 2y - 10 = 0\) ⇒ \(y = 5\), \(\min LRATC = 5^2 - 10 \cdot 5 + 30 = 5\).
2. **Equilibrium**: \(P = 5\), \(D(5) = 200 - 10 = 190\), so \(\# \text{firms} = \frac{190}{5} = 38\).
3. **Quasi-rent**: If \(P = 8\), each cafe earns \(\pi = 8 \cdot 5 - (5^2 - 10 \cdot 5 + 30) = 15\) (quasi-rent).

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Zero profit means firms are barely surviving."

Correction: Zero *economic* profit means firms earn normal returns (covering opportunity costs). Accounting profits may be positive.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Long-run equilibrium: \(P = \min LRATC\) and \(\pi = 0\).
- Efficient scale minimizes \(LRATC\).
- Rent compensates for input scarcity; quasi-rent is short-run surplus.
- Zero economic profit ≠ zero accounting profit.
