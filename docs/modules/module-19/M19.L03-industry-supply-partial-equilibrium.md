## Learning Objective

## Industry Supply and Partial Equilibrium

**Short-run industry supply** is the horizontal sum of individual firms' marginal cost curves (above AVC). In the **long-run**, supply depends on entry/exit:
- **Constant-cost industry**: Horizontal long-run supply (LRS) curve (e.g., identical firms with unlimited input availability).
- **Increasing-cost industry**: Upward-sloping LRS (e.g., specialized inputs become scarce as industry expands).
- **Decreasing-cost industry**: Downward-sloping LRS (e.g., external economies of scale like infrastructure improvements).

**Partial equilibrium analysis** studies market equilibrium (supply = demand) for one good, ignoring other markets.

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Consider the Australian wine industry. In the short-run, 100 identical wineries each have \(MC = 2y\). Market demand is \(D(p) = 1000 - 10p\).

1. **Short-run supply**: Each firm supplies \(y = p/2\), so industry supply is \(S(p) = 100 \cdot p/2 = 50p\).
2. **Equilibrium**: Set \(S(p) = D(p)\): \(50p = 1000 - 10p\) ⇒ \(p = 16.67\), \(y = 833.33\).
3. **Long-run**: If wine production increases labor costs (increasing-cost industry), LRS slopes upward.

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Industry supply curves are always upward-sloping."

Correction: Long-run supply can be horizontal (constant-cost) or downward-sloping (decreasing-cost).

---

## Key Takeaways

- Short-run industry supply sums firms' MC curves.
- Long-run supply depends on entry/exit and cost conditions.
- Partial equilibrium analyzes one market in isolation.
- External economies can cause decreasing-cost industries.
