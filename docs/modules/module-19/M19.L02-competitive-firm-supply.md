## Learning Objective

Derive a competitive firm's supply function from profit maximization.

---

## The Competitive Firm's Supply Decision

In a perfectly competitive market, firms are price takers and maximize profit by choosing output \(y\) such that price \(p\) equals marginal cost \(MC\): \(p = MC(y, w)\). The supply function \(y^*(p, w)\) is derived from this condition.

Key properties of the supply function:
- **Non-decreasing in \(p\)**: Higher prices incentivize greater output.
- **Homogeneous degree 0 in \(p, w\)**: Doubling all prices leaves output unchanged.

The **profit function** \(\pi(p, w)\) represents maximal profits and is convex in \(p\). **Hotelling's Lemma** states that \(\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial p} = y^*(p, w)\), linking profit to supply.

**Comparative statics**: How does \(y^*\) change with \(p\) and \(w\)?
- \(\frac{\partial y^*}{\partial p} \geq 0\) (upward-sloping supply).
- \(\frac{\partial y^*}{\partial w} \leq 0\) (higher input prices reduce supply).

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

A Melbourne bakery produces bread with cost function \(c(y) = 2y^2 + 10\). The market price is \(p = 24\) AUD/loaf.

1. **Supply decision**: Set \(p = MC\), where \(MC = \frac{dc}{dy} = 4y\). Thus, \(24 = 4y\) ⇒ \(y^* = 6\).
2. **Profit**: \(\pi = py - c(y) = 24 \cdot 6 - (2 \cdot 6^2 + 10) = 144 - 82 = 62\) AUD.
3. **Hotelling's Lemma**: Verify \(\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial p} = y^* = 6\).

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Firms always profit in competitive markets."

Correction: Firms earn zero economic profit in long-run equilibrium due to free entry/exit. Short-run profits can be positive, negative, or zero.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Competitive firms supply where \(p = MC\).
- Supply functions are non-decreasing in price and homogeneous degree 0.
- Hotelling's Lemma links profit and supply functions.
- Higher input prices reduce supply.

## Practice

2. Show that \(y^*(p, w)\) is homogeneous degree 0.
