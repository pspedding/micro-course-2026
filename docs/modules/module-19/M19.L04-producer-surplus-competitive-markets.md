## Learning Objective

Measure producer surplus and its relationship to profit in competitive markets.

---

## Producer Surplus and Profit

**Producer surplus (PS)** is the area above the supply curve and below the price: \(PS = TR - \text{Variable Costs}\). It equals total revenue minus the minimum amount firms would accept to produce \(y\).

In competitive markets:
- \(PS = \pi + \text{Fixed Costs}\) (since \(\pi = TR - \text{Total Costs}\)).
- **Welfare analysis**: Total surplus (consumer + producer) measures gains from trade. Competitive markets maximize total surplus (efficient).

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

A Brisbane firm has cost function \(c(y) = y^2 + 10\) and faces price \(p = 12\).

1. **Supply**: \(MC = 2y = p\) ⇒ \(y^* = 6\).
2. **PS**: Area above \(MC\) and below \(p\) is \(\int_0^6 (12 - 2y) dy = 12y - y^2 \big|_0^6 = 36\).
3. **Profit**: \(\pi = 12 \cdot 6 - (6^2 + 10) = 26\), so \(PS = 26 + 10 = 36\) (matches integral).

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Producer surplus equals profit."

Correction: Only true if fixed costs are zero; otherwise, \(PS = \pi + \text{Fixed Costs}\).

---

## Key Takeaways

- Producer surplus is the area above supply and below price.
- PS equals profit plus fixed costs.
- Competitive markets maximize total surplus.
- Efficiency requires \(P = MC\), which happens in competitive equilibrium.
