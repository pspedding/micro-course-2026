## Arrow-Pratt Measure

Absolute risk aversion:
ARA(c) = -U''(c)/U'(c)

Examples:
1. Constant ARA: U(c) = -e^{-αc}
2. Decreasing ARA: U(c) = ln(c)
3. Zero ARA: U(c) = c (risk neutral)

Australian health insurance:
- 55% have private hospital cover (APRA 2023)
- Medicare provides universal baseline

---

## Worked Example

**Australian context:**
Consider a farmer with:
- Initial wealth: $200k
- 20% chance of $80k drought loss
- Utility: U(c) = √c

1. Risk premium calculation:
EU = 0.2*√120k + 0.8*√200k ≈ 440.91
CE ≈ $194,400
Risk premium = $200k - $194.4k - $16k = $9.6k

2. Actuarially fair premium = 0.2*$80k = $16k

3. Maximum willingness to pay ≈ $25.6k

---

## Common Misconception

> "Insurance companies always profit from risk aversion"

Actually, adverse selection (e.g., sicker people buying more health insurance) can create market failures. Australia addresses this with:
- Lifetime Health Cover loading
- Private Health Insurance Rebate

---

## Key Takeaways

- Concave utility ⇒ risk aversion
- Insurance demand increases with risk aversion
- Actuarially fair insurance provides full coverage
- Australian dual system balances public/private
