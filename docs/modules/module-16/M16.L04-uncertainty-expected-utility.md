## Learning Objective

Evaluate choices under uncertainty using the expected utility framework.

---

## Expected Utility Theory

Von Neumann-Morgenstern utility function:
EU = π₁U(c₁) + π₂U(c₂)
Where:
- π = probabilities of states
- U(c) = utility from consumption

Key properties:
1. Risk aversion: U''(c) < 0 (concave)
2. Risk neutrality: U''(c) = 0 (linear)
3. Risk loving: U''(c) > 0 (convex)

---

## Worked Example

**Australian context:**
Consider flood insurance for a $500k home:
- 5% chance of total loss
- 95% chance no loss
- Premium: $30k

1. Expected value:
EV = 0.05*(-500k) + 0.95*(0) = -$25k

2. Risk premium:
For U(c)=√c, EU(no insurance) = 0.05*0 + 0.95*√500k ≈ 672.67
Certainty equivalent ≈ $452k
Risk premium = $500k - $452k - $25k = $23k

Australians pay ~$12b annually in general insurance premiums (APRA 2023).

---

## Common Misconception

> "People always choose the option with highest expected value"

Actually, risk-averse individuals may accept lower EV to reduce uncertainty (e.g., buying insurance).

---

## Key Takeaways

- Expected utility ≠ expected monetary value
- Risk preferences determine insurance demand
- State-contingent commodities allow hedging
- Australian insurance markets reflect risk aversion
