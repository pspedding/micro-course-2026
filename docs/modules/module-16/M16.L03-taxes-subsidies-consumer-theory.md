## Learning Objective

Compare the efficiency of different tax types using consumer theory.

---

## Tax Effects on Budget Constraints

1. **Quantity Tax (e.g., GST):** Rotates budget line inward
   - Example: 10% GST on good x makes px → 1.1px
   - Slope changes from -py/px to -py/(1.1px)

2. **Lump-Sum Tax:** Parallel shift inward
   - Reduces income but preserves relative prices
   - More efficient (less utility loss per dollar raised)

3. **Income Tax:** Similar to lump-sum but percentage-based
   - Australian marginal rates: 19%-45%

---

## Worked Example

**Australian context:**
Compare two ways to raise $1000 revenue from a consumer with:
- Income: $50,000
- Utility: U(x,y) = x^0.5 y^0.5
- Prices: px = $10, py = $20

1. **10% GST on x:**
   New px = $11 → Solve compensated demand
   DWL ≈ $120

2. **Lump-sum $1000:**
   M = $49,000 → Less distortion
   DWL ≈ $0

Australia's GST raises ~$75b/year vs income tax's ~$250b (ATO 2022-23).

---

## Common Misconception

> "All taxes equally distort consumer choices"

Actually, lump-sum taxes (though politically unpopular) are theoretically most efficient as they don't alter relative prices.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Quantity taxes distort relative prices
- Lump-sum taxes minimize deadweight loss
- GST vs income tax involves equity-efficiency tradeoffs
- Australian tax mix relies heavily on income taxes
