## Learning Objective

Derive an individual's labour supply curve from utility maximization under time constraints.

---

## The Labour-Leisure Tradeoff

Assume a worker has T total hours (e.g., 168/week) to allocate between:
- Leisure (R)
- Labour (L = T - R)

The budget constraint is:
wL + V = wT + V
Where:
- w = hourly wage (e.g., Australia's $23.23 minimum wage)
- V = non-labour income

The slope -w shows the opportunity cost of leisure in forgone wages.

---

## Worked Example

**Australian context:**
Consider a worker with:
- T = 100 hours/fortnight
- V = $200/fortnight (Centrelink payment)
- w = $25/hour

1. If they work 60 hours:
Income = 25*60 + 200 = $1700
Leisure = 40 hours

2. Optimal choice where MRS = w:
Maximize U(R, C) s.t. C = w(T-R) + V

At current Australian tax rates (19% above $18,200), the after-tax wage becomes critical.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Higher wages always increase labour supply"

Actually, when income effects dominate (at high wages), labour supply may decrease as people "buy" more leisure. Australia's workforce participation rate was 66.6% in Feb 2024 (ABS 6202.0).

---

## Key Takeaways

- The wage rate is the price of leisure
- Labour supply curves can bend backward
- Taxes create a wedge between gross and net wages
- Australian Fair Work Act sets minimum conditions
