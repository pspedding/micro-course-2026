## Learning Objective

Analyze how network effects shape technology markets and competition.

---

## Network Externalities

Network externalities occur when a product’s value increases with the number of users, leading to winner-take-all markets. Examples include EFTPOS (Australia’s payment network) and Afterpay (BNPL), where widespread adoption creates lock-in effects and high switching costs.

Tipping points can occur—once a platform reaches critical mass, competitors struggle to survive.

---

## Worked Example

**Afterpay’s Dominance in Australia**

1. **Early Adoption:** First-mover advantage attracted merchants and consumers.
2. **Network Effect:** More users made Afterpay more valuable for new participants.
3. **Lock-in:** Switching to competitors became costly due to established merchant partnerships.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Better technology always wins in competitive markets."

Network effects can lead to inferior technologies dominating (e.g., QWERTY keyboards). Market power often hinges on adoption speed, not just quality.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Network effects create winner-take-all dynamics.
- Positive feedback loops accelerate market dominance.
- Lock-in and switching costs protect incumbents.
- Competition policy must balance innovation with market power concerns.
