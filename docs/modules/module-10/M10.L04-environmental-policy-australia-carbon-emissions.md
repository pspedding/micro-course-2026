## Learning Objective

Evaluate Australia’s carbon pricing policies using microeconomic principles.

---

## Australia’s Carbon Policy Landscape

Australia’s carbon pricing has evolved from the 2012-14 carbon tax ($23/tonne) to the current Safeguard Mechanism (2023), which sets emissions baselines for large polluters. Fuel efficiency standards and renewable energy targets complement these market-based tools.

Political economy factors often shape policy design—e.g., the carbon tax’s repeal in 2014 due to industry lobbying.

---

## Worked Example

**Comparing Carbon Tax vs. Safeguard Mechanism**

1. **Carbon Tax (2012-14):** Fixed price per tonne, simple but politically contentious.
2. **Safeguard Mechanism (2023):** Baseline-and-credit system, flexible but complex to administer.
3. **Outcome:** Both aim to reduce emissions, but the mechanism allows firms more adaptability.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Market-based policies like carbon taxes hurt the economy."

Well-designed policies can reduce emissions while minimizing economic disruption. For example, revenue from carbon taxes can offset other taxes or fund transitions.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Australia has experimented with both carbon taxes and cap-and-trade systems.
- Policy stability is often disrupted by political factors.
- Fuel efficiency standards address transportation emissions.
- Economic efficiency depends on policy design and implementation.
