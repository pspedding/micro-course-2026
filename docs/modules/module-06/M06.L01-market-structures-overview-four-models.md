## Market Structures in Economics

Market structures describe the competitive environment in which firms operate. Economists classify markets into four main types based on:
1. Number of firms
2. Type of product (identical vs differentiated)
3. Degree of price control
4. Barriers to entry

In Australia, we can observe all four structures:
- **Perfect competition**: Grain markets (wheat, barley)
- **Monopolistic competition**: Cafés in Sydney
- **Oligopoly**: Major banks (CBA, Westpac, NAB, ANZ)
- **Monopoly**: Local utilities (Sydney Water)

---

## The Four Market Models

1. **Perfect Competition**
   - Many small firms
   - Identical products (wheat, shares on ASX)
   - No price control (price takers)
   - No barriers to entry/exit

2. **Monopolistic Competition**
   - Many firms
   - Differentiated products (restaurants, clothing)
   - Some price control
   - Low barriers (common in retail)

3. **Oligopoly**
   - Few large firms (Australian supermarkets: Woolworths, Coles)
   - Similar or differentiated products
   - Significant price control (interdependent pricing)
   - High barriers (capital requirements)

4. **Monopoly**
   - Single firm (Australia Post for standard mail)
   - Unique product
   - Complete price control (price makers)
   - Very high barriers (legal or natural)

## Worked Example

**Comparing Australian Banking and Agriculture**

1. Banking sector (oligopoly):
   - 4 major banks control ~75% market share
   - Similar products with branding differences
   - Follow each other's interest rate changes

2. Wheat farming (perfect competition):
   - Thousands of farmers
   - Standardized product (ASX-traded wheat)
   - Take global market prices

---

## Common Misconception

> "Monopolies always charge the highest possible prices"

While monopolies have price-setting power, they still face demand constraints. Australian monopolies like utilities have price caps set by regulators (ACCC) to protect consumers.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Market structures range from perfect competition to monopoly
- The number of firms and product differentiation determine competition
- Australian examples show real-world applications
- Barriers to entry affect long-term profitability
