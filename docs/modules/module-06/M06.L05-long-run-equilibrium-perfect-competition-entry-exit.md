## Learning Objective

Explain how entry and exit drive long-run equilibrium in perfect competition.

---

## The Adjustment Process

Perfect competition reaches long-run equilibrium through:

1. **Economic Profit → Entry**
   - New firms enter
   - Market supply increases
   - Price falls until profit = 0

2. **Economic Loss → Exit**
   - Firms exit
   - Market supply decreases
   - Price rises until loss = 0

Australian example: Solar panel installation market
- Early 2010s: High profits → surge of new installers
- By 2020: Prices stabilized → normal profits

---

## Long-Run Conditions

At equilibrium:
- P = MC = min ATC
- Firms earn **normal profit** (zero economic profit)
- Productive efficiency (min ATC)
- Allocative efficiency (P = MC)

Key distinction:
- Economic profit = 0
- Accounting profit > 0 (includes opportunity costs)

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Taxi Industry Post-Uber**

1. Pre-Uber:
   - Limited licenses → high P
   - Economic profits

2. Ride-sharing entry:
   - Supply increases
   - Prices fall
   - Traditional taxis exit

3. Long-run:
   - Normal returns for remaining operators
   - Lower prices for consumers

---

## Common Misconception

> "Zero economic profit means firms aren't making money"

Actually, normal profit means firms earn exactly their opportunity cost - enough to keep them in the industry. Owners still get fair compensation for their work and capital.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Entry/exit eliminate economic profits/losses
- Long-run equilibrium achieves dual efficiency
- Normal profit sustains business activity
- Real-world markets constantly adjust

---

## Extension: Monopolistic Competition — a Realistic Middle Ground

Perfect competition assumes identical products. Most real markets have **product differentiation** — restaurants, cafes, clothing brands, petrol stations. These are examples of **monopolistic competition**:

| Feature | Perfect competition | Monopolistic competition |
|---|---|---|
| Number of firms | Many | Many |
| Products | Identical | Differentiated |
| Entry/exit | Free | Free |
| Price | Taker (P = MR) | Maker (downward-sloping demand) |
| Long-run profit | Zero | Zero |

**Short run:** Each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve (like a mini-monopolist) and may earn positive profit.

**Long run:** Free entry attracts new firms → each firm's demand shrinks and becomes more elastic → entry continues until **P = ATC** (zero economic profit). Unlike perfect competition, P > MC still holds, so there is some residual inefficiency.

**Welfare trade-off — business stealing vs product variety:**
- *Business stealing effect:* A new entrant takes customers from existing firms — a negative externality the entrant ignores, suggesting *too many* firms may enter.
- *Product variety effect:* More firms mean more choice for consumers — a positive externality the entrant ignores, suggesting *too few* firms may enter.
- Net result: ambiguous. Monopolistic competition may have too many or too few firms; the model is honest about this uncertainty.

**Australian examples:** Independent cafes in Melbourne, petrol stations on a suburban strip, gyms, hairdressers.
