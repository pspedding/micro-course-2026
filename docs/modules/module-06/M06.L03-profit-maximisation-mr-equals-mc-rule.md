## Learning Objective

Apply the MR = MC rule to determine profit-maximizing output.

---

## The Golden Rule of Profit Maximisation

All firms, regardless of market structure, maximize profit where:

**Marginal Revenue (MR) = Marginal Cost (MC)**

Why this works:
- If MR > MC: Producing more adds to profit
- If MR < MC: Producing less reduces losses
- At MR = MC: No further profit gains possible

Australian context: This rule applies equally to:
- Small farms (perfect competition)
- Local cafes (monopolistic competition)
- Major banks (oligopoly)

---

## Four-Step Profit Analysis

1. **Find Q*** where MR = MC
2. **Find P** from demand curve at Q*
3. **Find ATC** from cost curve at Q*
4. **Calculate profit**: (P - ATC) × Q*

Example: Australian wheat farmer
- Market price: $300/tonne (P = MR)
- MC = $280 at Q* = 1000 tonnes
- ATC = $290 at Q*
- Profit = ($300 - $290) × 1000 = $10,000

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Café Economics**

1. Given:
   - P = $5 per coffee (price taker)
   - MC = $4 at Q* = 200 coffees/day
   - ATC = $4.50 at Q*

2. Profit calculation:
   - MR = P = $5
   - MR = MC at 200 units
   - Profit = ($5 - $4.50) × 200 = $100/day

3. Decision:
   - If demand falls to $4.25, should they operate?
   - Yes, if P > AVC (covers variable costs)

---

## Common Misconception

> "Firms should produce where MC is lowest"

Actually, firms produce where MR = MC, which may be at higher MC levels. Minimizing MC alone would lead to underproduction and lost profit opportunities.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Profit is maximized when MR = MC
- The rule applies universally across market structures
- Requires comparing revenue and cost at the margin
- Per-unit profit equals P - ATC
