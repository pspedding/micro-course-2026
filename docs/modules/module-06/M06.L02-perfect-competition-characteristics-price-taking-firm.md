## Learning Objective

Explain the five characteristics that define perfect competition.

---

## The Perfectly Competitive Market

Perfect competition represents an ideal market structure with maximum efficiency. While rare in pure form, some Australian markets approximate these conditions:

1. **Many small firms**: No single producer can influence market price (e.g., wheat farmers)
2. **Identical products**: Commodities where branding doesn't matter (ASX-listed shares)
3. **Free entry/exit**: No significant barriers (agricultural markets)
4. **Perfect information**: All participants know prices and quality (financial markets)
5. **Price takers**: Firms accept the market price (grain prices set globally)

---

## The Firm's Demand Curve

Key distinction:
- **Market demand**: Downward sloping (all consumers)
- **Firm demand**: Perfectly elastic (horizontal) at market price

Why? In perfect competition:
- P = AR = MR
- If a firm raises price → loses all customers
- If lowers price → unnecessary revenue loss

Australian example: Individual dairy farmers take the wholesale milk price set by the market.

---

## Worked Example

**ASX Share Trading**

1. Market characteristics:
   - Thousands of buyers/sellers
   - Identical shares of the same company
   - Real-time price information
   - No barriers to trading

2. Individual trader perspective:
   - Must accept current market price
   - No quantity restrictions
   - Can enter/exit positions instantly

---

## Common Misconception

> "Perfect competition means all firms earn zero profit"

In the long run, economic profit tends to zero due to entry/exit. However, firms can earn short-run profits or losses depending on cost structures and market price fluctuations (e.g., seasonal crop prices).

---

## Key Takeaways

- Perfect competition requires five specific conditions
- Firms are price takers facing horizontal demand
- Real-world markets may approximate some features
- Efficiency comes from inability to influence prices
