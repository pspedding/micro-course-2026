## Learning Objective

Analyze how price floors create surpluses and affect labor markets.

---

## Minimum Wage in Australia

The Fair Work Commission sets a national minimum wage ($23.23/hour as of 2023). This price floor ensures workers earn a living wage but can reduce employment opportunities, especially for low-skilled workers. The impact depends on labor demand elasticity: in competitive markets, higher wages may reduce jobs, but in monopsony markets, they can increase both wages and employment.

---

## Worked Example

**Minimum Wage and Employment**

1. **Equilibrium wage:** $20/hour, Quantity of labor = 1,000 workers.
2. **Minimum wage imposed:** $23.23/hour.
3. **Result:** Quantity demanded falls (fewer jobs), but quantity supplied rises (more people seek work). Surplus = 200 workers unemployed.
4. **Monopsony exception:** If the employer has market power, a minimum wage can increase employment.

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Minimum wages always reduce employment."**

In monopsony markets (single employer), a minimum wage can increase both wages and employment by countering employer power.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Price floors set above equilibrium create surpluses.
- Minimum wages can reduce employment in competitive markets but may increase it in monopsonies.
- Agricultural price floors (e.g., wheat boards) similarly create surplus produce.
- Elasticity of demand determines the employment impact.
