## Learning Objective

Explain how taxes affect market outcomes and why elasticity determines who bears the burden.

---

## Tax Incidence in Australia

A per-unit tax (e.g., fuel excise at ~49c/L) shifts the supply curve upward by the tax amount. Consumers pay a higher price, producers receive a lower net price, and the tax burden falls more heavily on the side with inelastic demand or supply. Deadweight loss occurs because the tax reduces mutually beneficial trade.

---

## Worked Example

**Fuel Excise Tax**

1. **Pre-tax equilibrium:** Price = $1.50/L, Quantity = 10M liters.
2. **Tax imposed:** $0.49/L.
3. **New equilibrium:** Consumer price = $1.80/L, Producer price = $1.31/L, Quantity = 8M liters.
4. **Incidence:** Consumers pay $0.30 more, producers receive $0.19 less. Deadweight loss = loss of 2M liters traded.

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Consumers always bear the full burden of taxes."**

Tax incidence depends on elasticity: if demand is elastic, producers bear more of the burden; if supply is elastic, consumers do.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Taxes create a wedge between consumer and producer prices.
- The more inelastic side bears more of the tax burden.
- Deadweight loss represents lost gains from trade.
- Australian examples include GST (broad-based) and tobacco taxes (targeted).
