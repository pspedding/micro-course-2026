## Learning Objective

Evaluate how subsidies alter market outcomes and create efficiency costs.

---

## Subsidies in Australia

Subsidies (e.g., childcare subsidies, renewable energy incentives) shift the supply curve downward, lowering consumer prices and increasing quantity. However, they can lead to overproduction and deadweight loss. The JobKeeper wage subsidy during COVID-19 aimed to preserve employment but also distorted labor markets.

---

## Worked Example

**Childcare Subsidy**

1. **Pre-subsidy equilibrium:** Price = $100/day, Quantity = 500 spots.
2. **Subsidy introduced:** $30/day.
3. **New equilibrium:** Consumer price = $80/day, Producer price = $110/day, Quantity = 600 spots.
4. **Welfare cost:** Deadweight loss from overproduction (some spots go unused).

---

## Common Misconception

> **"Subsidies always benefit society."**

While subsidies can make goods more affordable, they often lead to inefficient overproduction and fiscal costs.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Subsidies lower consumer prices and raise producer prices.
- They increase quantity but may cause overproduction.
- Deadweight loss occurs when the subsidy cost exceeds the benefit.
- Australian examples include renewable energy and childcare subsidies.

---
