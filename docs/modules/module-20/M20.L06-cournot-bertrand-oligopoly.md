## Learning Objective

Compare Cournot and Bertrand equilibrium outcomes using reaction functions.

---

## Cournot Model

Firms simultaneously choose quantities. Market clearing price P(Q) where Q = q₁ + q₂.

Firm 1's problem: max π₁ = P(q₁ + q₂)q₁ - C(q₁)

Australian example: Woolworths (W) and Coles (C) with P = 100 - Q, MC=10:

W's reaction: q_W = (100 - q_C - 10)/2 = 45 - 0.5q_C
C's reaction: q_C = 45 - 0.5q_W

NE: q_W = q_C = 30 → P = $40

---

## Bertrand Paradox

With homogeneous goods and MC₁ = MC₂ = c, P₁ = P₂ = c in equilibrium (competitive outcome).

Australian example: Petrol stations in Sydney undercut until P=MC ($1.50/L).

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Two Australian airlines (Qantas, Virgin) compete:

Case 1: Cournot with P=200-Q, MC=80
Case 2: Bertrand with differentiated products:
q_Q = 200 - 2p_Q + p_V
q_V = 200 - 2p_V + p_Q
MC=80

Find both equilibria.

Solution:
1. Cournot: q* = (200-80)/3 = 40 each, P=120
2. Bertrand: Solve reaction functions p_Q = (200 + p_V + 160)/4 etc → p_Q=p_V=120

---

## Common Misconception

> "More competitors always reduce prices"

With capacity constraints or product differentiation, prices may remain above MC even with many firms.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Cournot firms compete on quantities
- Bertrand firms compete on prices
- Product differentiation softens price competition
- Hotelling model explains spatial differentiation
