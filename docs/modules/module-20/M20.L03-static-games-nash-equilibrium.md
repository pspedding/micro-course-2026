## Learning Objective

Identify Nash equilibria in strategic form games using best response analysis.

---

## Nash Equilibrium Definition

A strategy profile (s₁*, s₂*) is a Nash Equilibrium if:

∀i, uᵢ(sᵢ*, s₋ᵢ*) ≥ uᵢ(sᵢ, s₋ᵢ*) for all sᵢ ∈ Sᵢ

Australian example: Two mining firms (BHP, Rio Tinto) choosing output:

|               | Rio: Low | Rio: High |
|---------------|----------|-----------|
| **BHP: Low**  | 50, 50   | 30, 60    |
| **BHP: High** | 60, 30   | 40, 40    |

NE: (High, High) - mutual best responses

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Find all NE in this Sydney cafe pricing game:

|            | Cafe B: $4 | Cafe B: $5 |
|------------|------------|------------|
| **Cafe A: $4** | 30, 30     | 50, 20     |
| **Cafe A: $5** | 20, 50     | 40, 40     |

Solution:
1. BR for A: If B=$4 → $4 (30>20); If B=$5 → $4 (50>40)
2. BR for B: If A=$4 → $4 (30>20); If A=$5 → $4 (50>40)
Only NE: ($4, $4)

---

## Common Misconception

> "Nash equilibrium means best possible outcome"

NE only means no unilateral incentive to deviate - often suboptimal (e.g., Prisoner's Dilemma).

---

## Key Takeaways

- NE requires mutual best responses
- Dominant strategies always played in NE
- Iterated elimination finds NE in dominance-solvable games
- Coordination games often have multiple NE
