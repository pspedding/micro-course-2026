## Learning Objective

Compute mixed strategy Nash equilibria using indifference conditions.

## Mixed Strategy Equilibrium

Player 1 plays U with probability p, D with (1-p)
Player 2 plays L with probability q, R with (1-q)

Equilibrium requires:
1. Player 2's mix makes Player 1 indifferent:
   q×u₁(U,L) + (1-q)×u₁(U,R) = q×u₁(D,L) + (1-q)×u₁(D,R)
2. Player 1's mix makes Player 2 indifferent:
   p×u₂(U,L) + (1-p)×u₂(D,L) = p×u₂(U,R) + (1-p)×u₂(D,R)

Australian example: Tennis serve (Federer vs Djokovic at Australian Open):

|            | Returner: L | Returner: R |
|------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Server: L** | 80, 20      | 30, 70      |
| **Server: R** | 20, 80      | 70, 30      |

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

Find mixed NE in this AFL penalty kick game:

|        | Goalie: L | Goalie: R |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Kicker: L** | 0.7, 0.3  | 0.9, 0.1  |
| **Kicker: R** | 0.8, 0.2  | 0.5, 0.5  |

Solution:
1. Goalie indifferent when:
   0.7p + 0.8(1-p) = 0.9p + 0.5(1-p) → p = 0.6
2. Kicker indifferent when:
   0.3q + 0.2(1-q) = 0.1q + 0.5(1-q) → q = 0.375
NE: Kicker aims L 60%, Goalie dives L 37.5%

## Common Misconception

> "Mixed strategies mean players randomize because they're uncertain"

Players randomize precisely to make opponents indifferent - a strategic requirement, not a reflection of uncertainty.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Mixed NE requires making opponents indifferent
- Probability weights balance expected payoffs
- Nash proved all finite games have at least one NE (possibly mixed)
- Applications in sports, security, auctions
