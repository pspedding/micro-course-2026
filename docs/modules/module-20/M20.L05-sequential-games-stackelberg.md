## Learning Objective

Solve sequential games using backward induction to find subgame perfect equilibria.

---

## Stackelberg Model

Leader (L) moves first, then follower (F). Solve backward:

1. F's problem: max π_F(y_F; y_L) → reaction function y_F(y_L)
2. L anticipates F's reaction: max π_L(y_L, y_F(y_L))

Australian example: BHP (leader) and Fortescue (follower) in iron ore market:

P = 100 - Q, MC_L = MC_F = 20

F's reaction: y_F = (100 - y_L - 20)/2 = 40 - 0.5y_L
L's profit: (100 - y_L - (40 - 0.5y_L) - 20)y_L → y_L* = 40
→ y_F* = 20

---

## Worked Example

**[Australian context]**

An Australian pharmaceutical firm (leader) and generic competitor (follower) face P = 200 - Q with MC=50. Find Stackelberg equilibrium.

Solution:
1. Follower: max (200 - y_L - y_F - 50)y_F → y_F = (150 - y_L)/2
2. Leader: max (200 - y_L - (150 - y_L)/2 - 50)y_L → y_L* = 75
   → y_F* = 37.5
   P* = 200 - 112.5 = $87.5

---

## Common Misconception

> "First movers always have advantage"

In some games (e.g., centipede game), first movers may be worse off due to credible threats.

---

## Key Takeaways

- SPNE requires optimal play in all subgames
- Backward induction eliminates non-credible threats
- Stackelberg leaders produce more than Cournot
- Commitment is key to first-mover advantage
