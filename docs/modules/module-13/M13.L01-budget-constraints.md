## Learning Objective

Derive and interpret the budget constraint equation for consumer choice problems.

---

## The Budget Constraint

A consumer with income m faces prices P₁ and P₂ for goods x₁ and x₂ respectively. The budget constraint is:

P₁x₁ + P₂x₂ ≤ m

The budget line (all bundles where the consumer spends exactly their income) is:

P₁x₁ + P₂x₂ = m

Rewriting to show intercepts:

x₂ = (m/P₂) - (P₁/P₂)x₁

- Vertical intercept (x₁=0): m/P₂
- Horizontal intercept (x₂=0): m/P₁
- Slope: -P₁/P₂ (opportunity cost of x₁ in terms of x₂)

**Australian Example:** Consider a student with $100 weekly budget for textbooks (P₁=$50) and coffee (P₂=$5). Maximum textbooks = 2, maximum coffees = 20.

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Budget Adjustment**

1. Original budget: 50x₁ + 5x₂ = 100
2. If textbook price rises to $60:
   - New intercepts: x₁ = 100/60 ≈ 1.67, x₂ = 20
   - New slope: -60/5 = -12 (steeper)
3. If income increases to $120:
   - New intercepts: x₁ = 120/50 = 2.4, x₂ = 24
   - Parallel shift outward

---

## Common Misconception

> "A price increase always reduces the maximum quantity of both goods"

Correction: Only the good whose price increased sees reduced maximum quantity. The other good's maximum remains unchanged (though affordable combinations change).

---

## Key Takeaways

- The budget line shows all combinations of goods that exhaust income
- Slope represents the market's tradeoff rate between goods
- Income changes cause parallel shifts
- Price changes rotate the budget line
- The numeraire good simplifies analysis by setting one price to 1
