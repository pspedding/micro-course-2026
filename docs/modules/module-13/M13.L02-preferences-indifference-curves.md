## Learning Objective

Construct indifference curves from preference relations and calculate marginal rates of substitution.

---

## Preference Relations

For any two bundles A and B:

- A ≿ B: "A is weakly preferred to B"
- A ≻ B: "A is strictly preferred to B"
- A ∼ B: "Indifferent between A and B"

An indifference curve shows all bundles yielding equal satisfaction.

Properties:
1. Downward sloping (more is better)
2. Cannot cross (transitivity)
3. Higher curves = more preferred
4. Diminishing MRS (convex to origin)

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS):
MRS = -dx₂/dx₁ along IC

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Housing Preferences**

Consider preferences between housing size (x₁) and commute time (x₂):

1. Given U = x₁^(0.6)x₂^(0.4)
2. MRS = -MU₁/MU₂ = -(0.6x₂)/(0.4x₁)
3. At bundle (100m², 30min):
   MRS = -(0.6*30)/(0.4*100) = -0.45
   → Willing to sacrifice 0.45min commute per extra m²

---

## Common Misconception

> "Indifference curves show actual consumption choices"

Correction: They represent potential tradeoffs - actual choices depend on budget constraints.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Indifference curves map equally preferred bundles
- MRS measures willingness to substitute between goods
- Well-behaved preferences are complete, transitive, monotonic, and convex
- Diminishing MRS reflects decreasing willingness to substitute
