## Learning Objective

Distinguish between major classes of utility functions and their implications for consumer choice.

---

## Special Preference Classes

1. **Perfect Substitutes**:
   - U = ax₁ + bx₂
   - Linear ICs, constant MRS = -a/b
   - Example: Different brands of rice

2. **Perfect Complements**:
   - U = min(ax₁, bx₂)
   - L-shaped ICs, MRS undefined at kink
   - Example: Left/right shoes

3. **Cobb-Douglas**:
   - U = x₁ᵃx₂ᵇ
   - Smooth convex ICs, MRS = -a/b × x₂/x₁
   - Example: General consumer goods

4. **Quasilinear**:
   - U = v(x₁) + x₂
   - Parallel ICs, MRS depends only on x₁
   - Example: Goods with no income effect

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Beverage Choices**

1. Perfect substitutes: Tea (x₁) and coffee (x₂) for caffeine:
   U = 2x₁ + x₂ → MRS = -2 always

2. Perfect complements: Coffee (x₁) and sugar (x₂) in fixed ratio:
   U = min(x₁, 5x₂) → 1 coffee : 5 sugars

3. Cobb-Douglas: U = √x₁√x₂ → MRS = -x₂/x₁

---

## Common Misconception

> "Cobb-Douglas preferences imply equal spending on both goods"

Correction: Spending shares equal exponent ratios (a:b), which needn't be 1:1.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Perfect substitutes have linear ICs and constant MRS
- Perfect complements have L-shaped ICs with optimal bundles at fixed ratios
- Cobb-Douglas yields smooth convex ICs with variable MRS
- Quasilinear preferences eliminate income effects for one good
