## Learning Objective

Transform utility functions while preserving preference orderings and calculate marginal rates of substitution.

---

## Utility Functions

A utility function U(x₁,x₂) assigns numbers to bundles such that:
U(A) > U(B) ⇔ A ≻ B

**Key Properties:**
1. Ordinal (only ranking matters)
2. Preserved under monotonic transformations
3. MRS = -MU₁/MU₂

**Common Forms:**
1. Cobb-Douglas: U = x₁ᵃx₂ᵇ
2. Perfect substitutes: U = ax₁ + bx₂
3. Perfect complements: U = min(ax₁, bx₂)

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Energy Preferences**

Consider U = √(electricity)×√(gas):

1. Show V = ln(U) represents same preferences:
   V = 0.5ln(x₁) + 0.5ln(x₂) → monotonic transformation

2. Calculate MRS:
   MU₁ = 0.5/√x₁, MU₂ = 0.5/√x₂
   MRS = -MU₁/MU₂ = -√x₂/√x₁

3. At (4,9): MRS = -3/2

---

## Common Misconception

> "Higher utility numbers mean greater happiness"

Correction: Utility is ordinal - only rankings matter, not absolute values.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Utility functions numerically represent preference orderings
- Monotonic transformations preserve preference rankings
- MRS depends only on preference structure, not utility scale
- Marginal utilities measure local tradeoff rates
