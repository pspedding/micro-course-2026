## Learning Objective

Analyze how preference properties shape indifference curves and consumer behavior.

---

## Fundamental Properties

1. **Completeness**: Can compare any two bundles (A≿B or B≿A)
2. **Transitivity**: If A≿B and B≿C, then A≿C
3. **Monotonicity**: More is better (if A has ≥ quantities than B, then A≿B)
4. **Convexity**: Averages preferred to extremes (if A∼B, then (A+B)/2 ≿ A)

**Special Cases:**
- Strict convexity: Averages strictly preferred
- Satiation: Bliss point exists

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Food Preferences**

Consider a consumer choosing between meat pies (x₁) and vegemite toast (x₂):

1. Show convexity holds for U=min(x₁, 2x₂)
   - Take A=(2,1) and B=(4,2) → U=2 for both
   - Average bundle C=(3,1.5) → U=min(3,3)=3
   - 3 > 2 → convexity satisfied

2. Show MRS is undefined at kinks (perfect complements)

---

## Common Misconception

> "Convex preferences mean the utility function is convex"

Correction: The utility function is quasiconcave - convex preferences correspond to convex lower contour sets.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Well-behaved preferences satisfy completeness, transitivity, monotonicity, and convexity
- Convexity implies diminishing MRS
- Strict convexity guarantees unique optimal bundles
- Non-monotonic preferences have bliss points
