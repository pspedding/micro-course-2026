## Learning Objective

Analyze why cartels form and why they often fail.

---

## Types of Collusion

- **Explicit collusion:** Formal agreements (e.g., cartels) to fix prices or output. Illegal in Australia under the Competition and Consumer Act.
- **Tacit collusion:** Unspoken coordination (e.g., price leadership by a dominant firm).

---

## Cartel Instability

Cartels collapse because:
1. **Incentive to cheat:** Members can profit by secretly undercutting the agreed price.
2. **Detection lag:** Cheating isn’t immediately noticed.
3. **Punishment:** If caught, firms face ACCC fines (e.g., Visy/Amcor cardboard cartel: $36M penalty).

---

## Worked Example: Visy/Amcor Cartel

**Scenario:** Rival packaging firms conspired to fix prices (2000s).
1. **Collusion:** Agreed to share markets and raise prices.
2. **Defection:** One firm secretly offered discounts to gain clients.
3. **Outcome:** ACCC investigation → massive fines and reputational damage.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Cartels are stable because members trust each other."

Cartels fail because trust is undermined by profit motives—the Prisoners' Dilemma in action.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Cartels are illegal and unstable due to cheating incentives.
- The ACCC detects and penalizes cartels to protect competition.
- Leniency policies reward whistleblowers (first to confess avoids fines).
- Tacit collusion is harder to regulate but still harmful.

---

## Practice

1. Why might a firm join a cartel despite the risks?
2. How does the ACCC’s leniency policy destabilize cartels?
3. Compare explicit and tacit collusion.
