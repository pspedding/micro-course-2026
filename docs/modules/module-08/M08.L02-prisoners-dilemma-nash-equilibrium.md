## Learning Objective

Explain why rational individuals might not cooperate, even when it benefits everyone.

---

## The Prisoners' Dilemma Setup

Two suspects (A and B) are arrested. Prosecutors lack evidence for a major charge but can convict on a minor charge (1 year). Each prisoner is offered:
- **Betray (Defect):** Go free if the other stays silent; serve 3 years if both betray.
- **Stay Silent (Cooperate):** Serve 1 year if the other betrays; serve 2 years if both stay silent.

Payoff matrix (years in prison, lower is better):

|                | B: Betray | B: Silent |
|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| **A: Betray**  | -3, -3    | 0, -3     |
| **A: Silent**  | -3, 0     | -2, -2    |

---

## Nash Equilibrium

A **Nash Equilibrium** occurs when no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing strategy. In the Prisoners' Dilemma, the Nash Equilibrium is (Betray, Betray) even though (Silent, Silent) is better collectively.

---

## Worked Example: ACCC and Cartels

**Scenario:** Two rival firms secretly agree to fix prices (cartel).
1. **Payoffs:**
   - If both comply, high profits.
   - If one defects (lower prices), they gain market share.
   - If both defect, profits plummet.
2. **Incentive:** Defect to undercut the rival (dominant strategy).
3. **Outcome:** Cartels collapse without enforcement (ACCC penalties).

---

## Common Misconception

> "Nash Equilibrium always leads to the best group outcome."

The Prisoners' Dilemma shows that Nash Equilibrium can be suboptimal due to individual incentives.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The Prisoners' Dilemma models conflict between individual and group rationality.
- Nash Equilibrium describes stable (but not always optimal) outcomes.
- Cartels are unstable because firms have incentives to cheat.
- The ACCC enforces anti-collusion laws to protect consumers.
