## Learning Objective

Describe how oligopolies balance competition and interdependence.

---

## Oligopoly Definition

An **oligopoly** is a market dominated by a few large firms (e.g., Australia's "Big 4" banks: CBA, Westpac, NAB, ANZ). Key traits:
- **High barriers to entry** (e.g., regulations, economies of scale).
- **Strategic interdependence** (firms react to rivals' actions).
- **Product differentiation** (e.g., brand loyalty in airlines like Qantas vs. Virgin).

---

## The Kinked Demand Curve Model

Explains price rigidity:
- If Firm A raises prices, rivals don’t follow → demand drops sharply.
- If Firm A lowers prices, rivals match → demand rises slightly.
Result: Prices "stick" at the kink to avoid profit losses.

---

## Worked Example: Australian Supermarkets

**Scenario:** Coles considers a 10% price cut on milk.
1. **If Woolworths ignores:** Coles gains market share.
2. **If Woolworths matches:** Minimal gain for Coles, profits drop for both.
3. **Likely outcome:** Woolworths matches → prices remain stable (kinked demand).

---

## Common Misconception

> "Oligopolies always collude to exploit consumers."

While collusion is illegal, tacit coordination (e.g., price leadership) can arise without explicit agreements.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Oligopolies feature mutual dependence among few firms.
- The kinked demand curve explains price stability.
- Collusion is illegal but tacit coordination occurs.
- Australian examples include banking, airlines, and supermarkets.
