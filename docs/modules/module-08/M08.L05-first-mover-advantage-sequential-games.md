## Learning Objective

Evaluate how timing affects strategic decisions in sequential games.

---

## Sequential vs. Simultaneous Games

- **Simultaneous games:** Players act without knowing rivals' choices (e.g., Prisoners' Dilemma).
- **Sequential games:** Players move in sequence, observing prior actions (e.g., Stackelberg model).

---

## First-Mover Advantage

The first player ("leader") can shape the game to their benefit:
- **Entry deterrence:** Incumbents invest heavily to discourage competitors (e.g., Australian mining firms securing port access).
- **Commitment devices:** Irreversible actions (e.g., Qantas pre-booking airport slots) force rivals to adapt.

---

## Worked Example: Airport Slot Allocation

**Scenario:** Two airlines (Qantas, Virgin) compete for Sydney Airport slots.
1. **Qantas moves first:** Secures prime departure times.
2. **Virgin responds:** Chooses less optimal slots → lower profitability.
3. **Outcome:** Qantas gains long-term advantage.

---

## Common Misconception

> "First movers always win."

First-mover advantage depends on the ability to sustain commitments (e.g., R&D costs). Late movers can sometimes leapfrog with innovation.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Sequential games are modeled using game trees (extensive form).
- First-mover advantage arises from credible commitments.
- Backward induction predicts rational play by working backward from outcomes.
- Australian examples include mining, aviation, and telecom.
