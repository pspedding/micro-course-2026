## Specialization and Trade

Countries gain by specializing in what they produce relatively more efficiently, even if another country is absolutely better at everything. Australia's economy demonstrates this through:
- Iron ore exports (30% of global supply)
- Agricultural exports (beef, wool, wine)
- Education services for international students

Absolute advantage means producing more with the same resources. Comparative advantage means lower opportunity cost. Trade allows both parties to consume beyond their PPFs.

---

## Worked Example

**Australia and China Trade**

1. Australia can produce either 100 tons of iron ore or 50 cars per year
2. China can produce either 80 tons of iron ore or 100 cars per year
3. Australia's opportunity cost: 1 car = 2 tons ore; China's: 1 car = 0.8 tons ore
4. Australia has comparative advantage in ore (lower opportunity cost)
5. China has comparative advantage in cars
6. Both benefit when Australia exports ore and imports cars

---

## Common Misconception

> "A country should only export goods it's the absolute best at producing"

Trade benefits come from relative (comparative) advantage, not absolute superiority. Australia exports education services despite not having the world's absolute best universities.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Comparative advantage depends on opportunity costs, not absolute productivity
- Specialization according to comparative advantage increases total output
- Trade allows consumption possibilities beyond a country's PPF
- Terms of trade determine how gains are distributed between trading partners
