## Learning Objective

Distinguish positive from normative economic statements.

---

## Economic Thinking

Positive economics describes what *is* - facts and testable relationships. Example:
- "Australia's inflation rate was 5.2% in 2023"
- "Higher interest rates reduce housing demand"

Normative economics prescribes what *should be* - value judgments. Example:
- "The RBA should prioritize employment over inflation control"
- "University should be free for all Australians"

Economic models simplify reality using assumptions like *ceteris paribus* (all else equal). The ACCC uses such models to analyze market competition.

---

## Worked Example

**Australian Housing Market Analysis**

1. Positive: "Sydney house prices fell 12% when interest rates rose 2%" (observable data)
2. Normative: "The government should intervene to stabilize prices" (policy opinion)
3. Model: Supply-demand curves assuming ceteris paribus (ignoring migration, wages, etc.)
4. Fallacy: "My suburb's prices rose, therefore nationwide prices are rising" (composition)

---

## Common Misconception

> "Economic models are useless because they're too simple"

Good models strip away complexity to reveal core relationships. The Reserve Bank's inflation models omit countless variables but still guide effective policy.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Positive statements are testable; normative involve value judgments
- Models simplify reality to highlight key relationships
- Ceteris paribus isolates variable effects by holding other factors constant
- Common fallacies: composition (part≠whole), post hoc (correlation≠causation)

---

## Practice

1. Classify: "Unemployment benefits reduce job-seeking" vs. "We should cut benefits"
2. Identify the fallacy: "My startup failed, therefore all small businesses fail"
3. Why might the ACCC assume ceteris paribus when analyzing Woolworths-Coles competition?
