## The Fundamental Economic Problem

Economics begins with the recognition that resources are limited while human wants are unlimited. In Australia, this plays out in everyday decisions: should we mine more iron ore in Western Australia or preserve the land? Should the RBA raise interest rates to control inflation or prioritize employment growth? These trade-offs exist because we can't have everything we want simultaneously.

The economic problem has three components:
1. What to produce (e.g., more housing vs. commercial buildings)
2. How to produce it (e.g., automated mining vs. labor-intensive methods)
3. For whom to produce (e.g., luxury goods for high-income earners vs. essentials for all)

---

## Worked Example

**Choosing between university and work**

1. Sarah finishes high school in Sydney and faces a choice: attend university or work full-time at Woolworths for $50,000/year.
2. If she chooses university, her opportunity cost is the $150,000 she could earn over 3 years plus any work experience.
3. If she chooses work, her opportunity cost is the higher lifetime earnings and career options from a degree (estimated $1 million extra over 40 years).
4. Rational choice suggests weighing these marginal benefits against costs.

---

## Common Misconception

> "Opportunity cost is just the monetary value of the next best alternative"

Opportunity cost includes all sacrificed alternatives - time, experiences, and non-monetary factors. Choosing to watch Netflix instead of studying costs both potential grades and the satisfaction of learning.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Scarcity exists because resources are limited relative to unlimited wants
- Every choice has an opportunity cost - what you give up when making a decision
- Economics studies how societies allocate scarce resources
- Positive economics describes what is, while normative economics argues what should be
